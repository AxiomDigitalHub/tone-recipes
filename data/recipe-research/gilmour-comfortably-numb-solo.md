## Recording context

- **Album:** *The Wall* (1979)
- **Studios:** Britannia Row Studios, London (initial sessions), CBS 30th Street Studio, NYC (overdubs), Producer's Workshop / Cherokee Studios, LA (final mix)
- **Producer:** Bob Ezrin + David Gilmour + Roger Waters
- **Engineer:** James Guthrie + Patrice Quef + Nick Griffiths
- Recording dates: April–November 1979

The two famous Comfortably Numb solos were tracked by Gilmour in single takes per solo. Bob Ezrin's instinct to pull Gilmour out of the band's rhythm-section layering and put him alone with a click track is what produced the second solo's vocal phrasing. Phil Taylor (Pink Floyd's longtime tech) was on hand for the rig setup.

## Guitar

- **Model:** Fender Stratocaster — "The Black Strat" (1969 alder-body Strat with a 1983 maple neck — though the neck was originally rosewood through The Wall era; the maple swap came in the 80s)
- **Pickup:** **Stock single-coil at the bridge** plus a **DiMarzio FS-1** in the neck position (added c. 1973). For the Comfortably Numb solos, Gilmour used the **bridge pickup** with the tone knob rolled back slightly.
  - Some sources cite the EMG SA active pickups — those came in 1985, AFTER The Wall. Don't conflate eras.
- **Tuning:** E standard, .010–.046 strings (Ernie Ball Slinky)
- **Strings:** GHS Boomers .010-.048 in the late 70s (later switched to .010-.046)
- **Notable mods:** Custom neck cut, "shortened" tremolo arm, black pickguard, white plastic pickup covers. The "Black" character comes from black pickguard over a sunburst body that Gilmour had refinished black.
- **Era caveat:** Many "Black Strat" pickups are post-1985 EMG SAs. For The Wall solos in 1979, it was passive single-coils + DiMarzio FS-1 neck. Don't use the EMG voicing.

## Pedalboard (chronological)

The Wall era pedalboard was housed in a Pete Cornish-built effects rack/board (the famous Cornish Black Box) — Cornish was Gilmour's main pedal builder from about 1976 onward.

| 1 | **MXR Dyna Comp** | Subtle compression for sustain | Sensitivity 5, Output 5 | Verified |

| 3 | **MXR Phase 100** (some sources) or **MXR Dyna Comp 2nd** | Modulation for verses (less common) | — | Likely |

| 5 | **MXR Digital Delay (M-117)** + **Binson Echorec II** in parallel | Delay (the iconic Gilmour wash) | Time ≈ 440 ms, Repeats ~ 4, Mix ~ 30 | Verified |
| 6 | **Electric Mistress Flanger** | Subtle modulation on some solo notes | — | Likely |

**Notes:**
- **The Big Muff is the solo fuzz.** Comfortably Numb solo 2 (the famous one) is bridge pickup → Big Muff Ram's Head → Hiwatt → cab. That's the chain.
- The CE-2 Chorus was used for the verses' clean tone, NOT the solo. The solo has no chorus; the swirling quality comes from the studio's plate reverb + Yamaha RA-200 rotating speakers (live).
- **Studio vs. live differs:** Live, Gilmour added the Yamaha RA-200 rotating speaker cabinet for solos — that's what gives the "Pulse" version its swirling 3D quality. The studio recording does NOT include the RA-200.
- The Electric Mistress was on the board but used very sparingly on Comfortably Numb solo specifically.

## Amp + Cab

- **Amp:** Hiwatt DR-103 Custom 100 (single head, Normal channel)
  - Channel: Normal only (Brilliant channel was zeroed)
  - Settings: Normal Volume 5, Brilliant Volume 0, Bass 4, Treble 6, Presence 6, Master 7
  - The Hiwatt DR-103 is a 100W head, all-tube, point-to-point hand-wired
- **Cab:** WEM (Watkins Electric Music) 4x12 cabinets
  - **Speakers:** Fane Crescendo 100W speakers (NOT Celestion). The Fanes are a critical part of the open, hi-fi character — much smoother than Celestions, less midrange honk, more linear high end
- **Power tubes:** EL34 quad
- **Modifications:** Stock — Hiwatts are notoriously over-built and don't typically need modding
- **Multi-cab setup:** Single 4x12 in the studio. Live, Gilmour ran multiple Hiwatt DR-103s + WEM cabs + the RA-200 rotating speakers in stereo.

The Hiwatt + WEM/Fane combination is the polar opposite of the Marshall-Plexi-Greenback recipe. Marshall+Greenbacks compress and saturate aggressively; Hiwatt+Fanes stay clean and hi-fi, letting the Big Muff do all the gain work in front. THIS is why the Comfortably Numb solo sounds so vocal — the amp doesn't add its own coloration on top of the fuzz.

## Microphones

- **Close mic:** Shure SM57 — straight-on, 1" off the speaker grille
- **Off-axis / second mic:** Sennheiser MD421 — close, off-axis ~30°, blended with the SM57
- **Distant mic:** Possibly a Neumann U87 at 4–6 ft for room ambience (James Guthrie's preferred technique on rock guitar)
- The Wall mixes were tracked at multiple studios so the exact mic chain varies by session

## Technique notes

- **Right-hand attack:** Gilmour uses a thick pick (1.0 mm + Herco gold or similar) and digs in firmly on solo notes. The pick attack is a major contributor to the singing tone.
- **Vibrato:** SLOW, wide vibrato — Gilmour's vibrato is closer to a violinist's than most guitarists'. Each note holds for a count before the vibrato starts.
- **Bending:** Always bends UP from a target — never overshoots. The "vocal" quality is all about controlled bend speed.
- **Volume swells:** Used heavily on cleaner sections (e.g., "Shine On You Crazy Diamond" intro), less so on Comfortably Numb solo.
- **Tone knob roll-off:** Slight (8–9 instead of 10) to take edge off the Big Muff's high end. NOT the rolled-back "woman tone" Clapton uses.

## Open questions

- **Did the Yamaha RA-200 rotating speaker fire on the studio Comfortably Numb solo?** Most sources say NO — the RA-200 was a live-only addition starting around 1980+ tours. The studio solo's swirl is purely plate reverb. But some sources are ambiguous.
- **MXR Digital Delay vs. Binson Echorec — which is on the recording?** Both were on the board. Most consensus suggests the digital delay was the primary on Comfortably Numb (the repeats are too clean to be a tape Echorec). The Echorec was more central on Time / Wish You Were Here.
- **Pickup tone roll-off exact position?** Some sources say full bridge open; others say tone at 8 or 7 to soften the Big Muff's high end. Probably player-by-player adjustment.
