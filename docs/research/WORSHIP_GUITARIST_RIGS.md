### 1. Nigel Hendroff (Hillsong Worship / Hillsong United)

**Overview**: Nigel Hendroff is widely regarded as the most influential worship electric guitarist of the modern era. His tone has defined the Hillsong sound from approximately 2004 onward, appearing on virtually every major Hillsong worship album during that period. He has a Gretsch signature model (the G6134TFM-NH Signature Penguin) and developed the "Droff VX15" Kemper profile pack with MultiTracks.com.

#### Guitars
- **Gretsch G6134TFM-NH Signature Penguin** (Amber Flame and Dark Cherry Metallic Flame) — his primary instrument
- **Gretsch White Falcon** — used for specific tonal colors
- **Gretsch G6112TCB-JR Center-Block LTD** — 2-tone, smaller body
- **Duesenberg Starplayer TV** (Blue Pearl with tremolo) — used extensively, especially in earlier years
- **Fender Telecaster** — for brighter, more cutting tones
- **Various Duesenberg models** — he has been a long-time Duesenberg player

**Key guitar insight**: Hendroff gravitates toward bright, chimey guitars with single coils or Filter'Tron pickups. The Gretsch Filter'Tron tone is central to his sound — that "glossiness, chime, and mid-range" that cuts through a worship mix.

#### Amps
- **English-made Vox AC15** — the cornerstone amp of nearly every Hillsong worship album he played on since 2004. EL84 tubes provide the chimey, sweet midrange character.
- **Vox AC30** — he "absolutely loves the AC30"
- **Matchless DC-30 2x12 Combo** — used on the "No Other Name" tour (2014)
- **Matchless Spitfire 15** — documented in his 2009 rig
- **Quilter Tone Block 200** — a lightweight solid-state option for travel/fly dates
- **Jackson amplifiers** — used alongside Vox and Matchless

**Amp philosophy**: Semi-clean to edge-of-breakup. He pushes the front end with a preamp pedal rather than cranking the amp. The amp stays relatively clean while the drive comes from stacked pedals.

#### Pedalboard (Signal Chain)
Controlled by an **RJM Mastermind PBC/10** loop switcher. The board is large and complex, with custom buffered/isolated loops and custom cables.

**Always-on foundation pedals:**
1. **Jackson Audio Prism** — preamp/boost, always on. He describes this as the foundation of his sound, pushing the signal into the amp harder.
2. **Jackson Audio Bloom** — compressor/EQ, always on alongside the Prism.

**Drive section:**
3. **Mulholland Drive** — used sparingly for extreme drive situations. Layered on rare occasions.
4. Various other overdrive pedals (rotated over the years)

**Modulation / Time-based:**
5. **Strymon Mobius** — modulation effects
6. **Strymon Timeline** — primary delay
7. **Strymon BigSky** — reverb with custom short, medium, and long reverb presets
8. **Eventide Space** — additional reverb textures
9. **Eventide H9 Max** — multi-effects for specialized sounds
10. **Selah Effects Quartz Timer** — tap tempo/clock for syncing delays

**Ambient preset**: He has an RJM preset that activates approximately 6 pedals simultaneously — long reverb, fancy delay setting, reverse effect, and a drive — for ambient swells and pad-like textures.

#### Tone Philosophy
- Bright guitar + chimey amp + dynamic playing = the Hillsong guitar sound
- He shapes tone primarily with his right hand attack and how hard he hits the strings
- The preamp pedal (Prism) is always on, pushing the amp input for more harmonic richness
- Dynamics are paramount — the volume knob and pick attack do most of the tonal work
- Delay and reverb create space, but the core tone is about the guitar-to-amp relationship

#### Rig Evolution
- **~2004-2009**: Duesenberg Starplayer TV, Matchless Spitfire 15, smaller pedalboard
- **~2009-2014**: Expanded to include Gretsch guitars, Matchless DC-30, added Strymon and Eventide units, RJM switching system
- **~2014-2020**: Gretsch Signature Penguin, AC15/AC30 focus, Quilter for travel, Jackson Audio pedals become central
- **~2020+**: Kemper profiles (Droff VX15), continued refinement of the core Gretsch/Vox/chimey approach

#### Signature Techniques
- Dotted-eighth delay picking patterns
- Volume swells using guitar volume knob
- Rhythmic strumming with heavy use of dynamics
- Ambient pad-like textures using stacked reverb and delay
- Clean arpeggiated picking with shimmer

### 2. The Edge (U2) — The Worship Guitar Godfather

**Overview**: While not a worship musician himself, The Edge's approach to delay-driven guitar has been the single most influential force on modern worship guitar tone. His techniques — dotted-eighth delays, rhythmic picking through delay, ambient textures — were directly adopted by the entire worship guitar movement.

#### Guitars
- **1976 Gibson Explorer** — his most iconic guitar, natural-finish korina wood. Purchased on U2's first trip to New York City. Used on countless recordings.
- **1973 Fender Stratocaster** — DiMarzio FS-1 pickup in bridge position. Essential for bright, articulate tones on songs like "Where the Streets Have No Name" and "Bad."
- **Gibson Les Paul Custom** (various) — used selectively
- **Fender Telecaster** (various) — used on specific songs
- **Gibson SG** — used in earlier U2 material
- **Gretsch Country Gentleman** — used on some recordings
- **Custom Fender Stratocasters** — various, including signature models
- On the 360 degree Tour, he traveled with 45 guitars total, using 18-20 different instruments each show

#### Amps
- **1964 Vox AC30TB** (Grey Panel, Jensen Blue Alnico 12" speakers) — used on every U2 album. The chimey, complex harmonics from this amp are synonymous with his sound.
- **Two 2015 Vox AC30TB combos** — handwired by Dave Clarke for touring
- **Two 2016 Vox AC30s** — also tweaked by Dave Clarke
- **Fender "Edge" Deluxe** — custom Fender collaboration
- **1957 Fender Tweed Deluxe** — used for specific tones
- **1957 Fender Harvard** — used in studio

**Key amp insight**: The Vox AC30 is THE amp. Everything else is supplementary. The AC30's top boost circuit with its chimey, harmonically rich character is the foundation.

#### Effects / Delay Rig

The Edge's effects are controlled by **two Bob Bradshaw RS40 controllers** — one onstage, one with his guitar tech Dallas Schoo.

**Core Delay Units:**
1. **Electro-Harmonix Deluxe Memory Man** — analog delay that shaped U2's early sound (Boy, October). Bucket-brigade circuit produces slightly degraded, darker repeats.
2. **Korg SDD-3000** — became primary from The Unforgettable Fire onward. Precise delay times, programmable presets, slightly filtered/modulated repeats. Modulation rate at 5Hz, depth until repeats wobble. High-frequency filter set to cut above 4kHz, low cut below 200Hz.
3. **TC Electronic 2290** — alongside SDD-3000 for cleaner, more precise digital delays. Uses sine wave LFO (no triangle option). Multiple units running simultaneously.

**Typical Delay Configuration:**
- Multiple delays running simultaneously — dotted eighth + quarter note, or slapback + longer rhythmic delay
- For "Bad," "Streets," "Pride": TC2290 set to eighth note + another TC2290 set to dotted eighth
- Dotted eighth note delay is THE signature setting
- Each repeat gets slightly darker (high-frequency rolloff) and the low end is filtered to prevent mud

**SDD-3000 Settings:**
- Modulation rate: 5Hz (knob position ~6)
- Modulation depth: turned up until repeats wobble (~1.5 on knob)
- High filter: 4-8 kHz
- Low filter: 250 Hz

**Rack Effects:**
- Juice Goose Power Rack 200
- Custom Audio Electronics Dual Stereo Line Mixer
- Rocktron Patchmate (switching)
- Rocktron DVC Dynamic Volume Controller
- Korg A3 (multi-effects)
- Yamaha SPX100
- Line 6 Pod Pro

**Stompbox Effects:**
- Boss Equalizer GE-7
- Boss Super Overdrive SD-1
- Boss Turbo Overdrive OD-2
- Boss Compression Sustainer CS-2
- Dunlop Dallas Fuzz Face
- TC Electronic Preamp

#### How Worship Guitarists Adapted His Approach
- **Dotted-eighth delay** became the defining worship guitar technique — a single guitarist can fill an entire room with rhythmic, ambient texture
- The concept of "delay as a rhythmic instrument" rather than just an effect
- Volume-based dynamics — playing softly with delay creates ambient washes, playing harder creates rhythmic patterns
- Minimalist note choice — The Edge picks simple patterns and lets delay create complexity
- The AC30 became the worship amp standard directly because of The Edge's influence
- His philosophy of serving the song rather than showing off guitar chops aligned perfectly with worship theology

#### Playing Style
- Primarily pick-based with very deliberate, rhythmic picking
- Simple note choices — delay does the heavy lifting
- Dynamics are perhaps his most important "effect"
- Extensive use of harmonics and open strings
- Guitar volume knob constantly in use

### 3. Martin Smith / Stu G (Delirious?)

**Overview**: Delirious? (1992-2009) were arguably the most influential UK worship band ever. Martin Smith was the frontman/vocalist who also played guitar, while Stu Garrard (Stu G) was the lead guitarist who shaped the band's guitar sound. Together they influenced an entire generation of UK and global worship guitarists. Songs like "I Could Sing of Your Love Forever," "History Maker," "Shout to the North," and "Majesty" became standards.

#### Stu G's Guitars (Lead Guitarist)
- **Fender Player Telecaster** (1990s Mexican-made) — purchased for 199 GBP in 1993, extensively modified over the years with Bill Lawrence vintage Tele pickups, new bone nut, and new bridge. His primary workhorse for years.
- **Gibson Les Paul Standard** (Red Sunburst) — kept in the UK
- **Gibson Les Paul Gold Top Deluxe** — kept in the UK
- **Gibson Les Paul Standard** (Black) — kept in the USA
- **Veritas Signature Guitar** — custom signature model

#### Stu G's Amps
- **Kemper Profiler** — current primary amp platform (used with Michael W. Smith's band)
- During the Delirious? era, likely Vox and Marshall-style amps (specific models not extensively documented)

#### Stu G's Pedalboard
Controlled by a **GigRig G2 switcher** with Humdinger buffer.

Key pedals:
- **JHS Kilt V2** — collaboration between Stu G and JHS Pedals, based on a Bixonic Expandora he used during the Delirious? "Metamorphasis" era
- **Walrus Audio Iron Horse** — RAT-type distortion, used for 80s/90s material with Michael W. Smith
- **Two Eventide H9 pedals** — one for reverbs and tremolo, one dedicated to delays only
- **JHS pedals** (various) — part of his core overdrive/boost section
- **Walrus Audio pedals** (various) — used throughout the board

#### Martin Smith's Gear
Martin Smith's specific guitar gear is less documented than Stu G's, as he was primarily known as the vocalist/songwriter. He played rhythm guitar and acoustic guitar, with some electric guitar work.

- Known to use **Fender** and **Gibson** guitars
- His Equipboard profile exists but with limited verified gear data

#### The Delirious? Sound
The guitar sound on landmark recordings came from Stu G's rig and Martin's rhythm work together:
- "History Maker" — driving rock energy with crunchy overdriven guitar
- "I Could Sing of Your Love Forever" — clean, chimey arpeggiated guitar with delay
- "Majesty" — layered electric guitar with dynamic builds
- "Rain Down" — ambient, delay-heavy textures

#### Influence
- Defined the UK worship guitar sound of the late 1990s and 2000s
- Showed that worship music could have rock-level production and guitar tone
- Stu G's combination of Les Paul grit and Telecaster chime influenced a generation
- The JHS Kilt pedal is a direct artifact of the Delirious? tone
- Stu G currently tours with Michael W. Smith, continuing to influence worship guitar globally

### 4. Jad Gillies (Hillsong United)

**Overview**: Jad Gillies was a foundational guitarist for Hillsong United during their formative years. Alongside Nigel Hendroff, he helped establish the Hillsong guitar sound that would become the global standard. His rig documentation comes primarily from around 2009.

#### Guitars
- **Fender Telecaster '52 Reissue** (2005, Blonde) — primary electric
- **Fender Stratocaster Plus** (1990, 3-Color Sunburst) — secondary electric
- **Gibson Les Paul** (2002, Black/Ivory) — for heavier tones
- **Gibson Songwriter Deluxe** — acoustic
- **Maton M320** — acoustic (Maton is an Australian brand, very common in Hillsong)

#### Amps
- **Mesa Boogie Nomad 55W Head** — primary amp
- **1974 Fender Twin Reverb** — clean platform
- **Marshall 1960A 4x12 cab** — paired with the Mesa head
- **Radial Bigshot ABY** — amp switching between the two amps

#### Pedalboard (Signal Chain Order, circa 2009)
1. Boss TU-2 Tuner
2. Dunlop Classic Wah
3. MXR Micro Amp (boost)
4. Xotic RC Booster
5. Voodoo Labs Sparkle Drive
6. Ibanez Tube Screamer
7. Visual Sound Jekyll & Hyde (overdrive/distortion)
8. Cusack Screamer Fuzz
9. Rocktron Hush (noise gate)
10. Ernie Ball VP Junior (volume)
11. Boss DD-5 Digital Delay (x2)
12. TC Electronic Nova Delay
13. True Bypass Switcher

**Cabling**: George L's patch cables, Monster Cable "Monster Bass" instrument cables

**Power**: Visual Sound One Spot

#### Notable Recordings
- Jad was a core part of the Hillsong United guitar team during their rise
- His credits span across multiple Hillsong United albums
- He helped define the layered guitar approach where multiple guitarists create a wall of sound

#### Key Insight
Jad's rig was more traditional rock-oriented compared to Nigel's chimey approach — the Mesa/Fender/Marshall combination with Tube Screamer stacking is more classic rock-influenced. This diversity within Hillsong's guitar team (Nigel's chime + Jad's grit) created the full Hillsong guitar sound.

### 5. Matt Crocker (Hillsong United)

**Overview**: Matt Crocker is a worship leader, songwriter, and multi-instrumentalist for Hillsong United and Hillsong Worship. He is known primarily as a vocalist and songwriter ("What a Beautiful Name," "O Praise the Name") but also plays guitars and percussion.

#### Known Gear
- Plays guitars and percussion for Hillsong United
- **Fender Jazzmasters and Telecasters** are commonly seen in Hillsong United's guitar section (general Hillsong preference)
- Specific pedalboard details are not publicly documented in detail
- His Instagram (@mattycrocker) occasionally shows gear in live settings

#### Approach to Worship Guitar
- Matt Crocker approaches guitar as a worship leader first, instrumentalist second
- His guitar work tends to be supportive and textural rather than lead-focused
- He serves the song and the congregational experience
- As a songwriter, his guitar parts are designed to be playable by church guitarists

#### Influence
- As the writer of some of the most-played worship songs globally, his guitar arrangements directly influence what thousands of church guitarists play each week
- His songwriting approach — simple but powerful chord progressions with dynamic builds — has shaped modern worship structure

### 6. Brad Ligon (Elevation Worship)

**Overview**: Elevation Worship's guitar section has been influential in shaping the current worship guitar sound. Brad Ligon and the Elevation guitar team are known for polished, layered guitar tones that blend ambient textures with driving rock energy.

- Layered guitars with distinct roles (rhythm, lead, ambient/pad)

- Dynamic builds from sparse, clean picking to full, driven strumming

### 8. Jeffrey Kunde (Jesus Culture)

**Overview**: Jeffrey Kunde is the lead guitarist for Jesus Culture and one of the most well-documented worship guitarists in terms of gear. His rig is comprehensive and has been featured in multiple detailed rundowns.

#### Guitars
- **Gretsch Duo Jet** — primary electric
- **Gretsch White Falcon** — secondary
- **Fender Telecaster American Standard** — for Tele tones

#### Amps
- **1993 Vox AC30 6TB** with original vintage speakers — primary
- **Matchless DC-30** — secondary
- **Jackson Britain 3.0** — boutique option
- **Tioga Ezekiel** — newer addition

#### Pedalboard (Full Signal Chain)
Controlled by **RJM Mastermind PBC** with **Selah Effects Quartz** (tap/clock) and **Sonic Research Turbo Tuner Mini**.

Signal chain:
1. **Bondi Effects Del Mar** — always-on clean boost at front of chain
2. **Keeley Compressor** (2-knob) — level at 2-3 o'clock, sustain mostly down
3. **Matthew Effects Modified EHX Nano POG** — octave, smaller footprint with preset function
4. **Chase Bliss Audio Gravitas** — tremolo
5. **Chase Bliss Audio Spectre** — flanger
6. **Walrus Audio Jupiter** — fuzz (versatile bright-to-dark range)
7. **Analog delay** (darker character with sweet modulation)
8. **Electro-Harmonix Memory Man** — classic analog delay
9. **Strymon Timeline** — primary digital delay
10. Additional reverb/ambient effects

Board built into a **Pelican 3100 Series** case, configured in two tight rows.

#### Key Insight
Kunde's rig represents the "full-featured" worship pedalboard — extensive switching, multiple delay types (analog + digital), fuzz capability, octave effects, and boutique drive pedals. His Gretsch/AC30 foundation is the quintessential modern worship guitar platform.

### 9. Jimmy Abegg / Rick Elias (Ragamuffin Band)

**Overview**: Jimmy Abegg and Rick Elias were the guitarists in Rich Mullins' A Ragamuffin Band, assembled in 1993. Their playing represented a more organic, less effects-driven approach to worship-adjacent music — raw, emotional, and serving the song.

#### Jimmy Abegg
- Multi-instrumentalist: electric guitar, acoustic guitar, mandolin
- Previously a founding member of the progressive rock band **Vector** (Sacramento, CA, 1985)
- Also a member of **Charlie Peacock's Acoustic Trio**
- Visual artist who created album artwork for Phil Keaggy, Michael W. Smith, and others
- Based in Nashville
- Specific gear details are not well-documented, but his playing style was rooted in Telecaster-style tone with emphasis on feel and dynamics over effects

#### Rick Elias
- Played vocals, guitar, and keys in the Ragamuffin Band
- Also a prolific producer for Amy Grant, Rich Mullins, Aaron Neville, Michael W. Smith, and Randy Stonehill
- Born January 7, 1955; passed away April 2, 2019
- Specific gear details are not publicly documented

#### Influence
- Represented an alternative approach to worship guitar — less about effects and production, more about raw emotion and musical integrity
- Rich Mullins' music required guitarists who could serve simple, powerful melodies without overplaying
- Their work predated the "worship pedalboard" era and influenced worship musicians who prioritize musicianship over gear

### 10. Bob Hartman (Petra)

**Overview**: Bob Hartman founded Petra in 1972, making it one of the earliest and most influential Christian rock bands. His guitar work defined Christian rock guitar for over two decades.

#### Guitars
- **Gibson Les Paul Classic** — primary rock guitar
- **Carvin DC 400C** — alternate electric
- **PRS Santana SE II** (Cherry Red)
- **Ibanez Talman**
- **Fender American Standard Stratocaster**
- **Gretsch Roundup**
- **Zion Powerglide**
- **Gibson Les Paul Junior**
- Also builds his own guitars using **Warmoth** parts with Wilkinson tremolo and Sperzel locking tuners

#### Amps
- **Marshall JCM 2000 TSL 100** (Triple Super Lead) — documented live amp
- Marshall amps were central to Petra's heavier sound

#### Effects
- **Boss GT-6** multi-effects processor — visible in live performance footage
- Used chorus on lead tones during certain eras
- Relatively straightforward effects approach compared to modern worship rigs

#### Playing Style
- Classic rock-influenced: Hendrix, Clapton, Beck, Page, Joe Walsh
- More planned/composed approach to solos (contrasted with Pete Orta who was more spontaneous)
- Solid rhythm work with melodic lead lines

#### Influence
- Proved that Christian music could have legitimate rock guitar
- Influenced an entire generation of church guitarists in the 1980s-1990s who grew up on Petra
- His tone was Marshall-driven rock — a completely different paradigm from modern worship guitar

### 11. Oz Fox & Michael Sweet (Stryper)

**Overview**: Stryper's dual-guitar attack of Oz Fox and Michael Sweet brought arena-level metal guitar to Christian music. Their yellow-and-black aesthetic and technical playing influenced Christian hard rock and metal guitarists.

#### Oz Fox — Guitars
- **GMW Custom Guitars** — his primary instruments throughout career
- Custom "Chevron" designs
- All guitars feature a special coil-tap switch that takes a coil from each humbucker and puts them out of phase, creating the distinctive "metallic grind" in Stryper's tone
- **Seymour Duncan pickups** in most guitars

#### Oz Fox — Amps
- **Mesa/Boogie Mark II-C** — used on Soldiers Under Command and To Hell With the Devil (their two most successful albums)
- **Mesa/Boogie Mark V 90W** — current primary amp
- **Mesa 400 power amp** — used with modeler for stage volume
- **Mesa 4x12 cabs** (x2)

#### Oz Fox — Effects
- **Line 6 POD X3 Live** — primary tone source in recent years (any amp on stage serves as power amp only)
- **MXR EQ pedal** — everything flat with 2kHz slightly boosted
- **Digitech effects** — chorus with 26ms delay between cabs
- **Noise gates** — essential due to high-gain front-end boosting
- Historically used delay live (notably on the THWTD tour)

#### Michael Sweet — Guitars
- **Jackson Custom Shop Reverse Rhoads** — signature design
- **Carvin custom guitars** (24 frets, Seymour Duncan Blackout active pickups) — replicas of the Randy Rhoads design
- **PRS Custom 24** with Blackout pickups

#### Michael Sweet — Amps
- **Mesa/Boogie Mark V** — primary
- **Splawn heads** (x2) — alternate
- **Orange Thunderverb 200** — used in recording alongside Mesa

#### Michael Sweet — Effects
- **ISP Technologies THETA PRO DSP** (MS Signature) — all-in-one: pre-EQ, distortion, post-EQ, effects, noise reduction, global EQ
- **Tech 21 Para Driver** — parametric EQ driving the front end
- **Radial London Bones** — distortion pedal

#### Stryper Tone Recipe
- Mesa Mark series amp + mid-boost EQ + coil-tapped humbuckers = the Stryper crunch
- High-gain with mid-frequency emphasis
- Noise gates are essential at these gain levels

### 12. Dana Key (DeGarmo & Key)

**Overview**: Dana Key (December 30, 1953 — June 6, 2010) was co-founder of DeGarmo & Key with keyboardist Eddie DeGarmo. They were best friends since first grade and became one of the most successful Christian rock acts of the 1980s. Key was a descendant of Francis Scott Key.

#### Known Gear
- Specific detailed gear documentation is limited
- The DeGarmo & Key sound was rooted in **classic rock and AOR** (Album-Oriented Rock) conventions
- Based on the era and genre, likely used:
  - Gibson and Fender guitars
  - Marshall amplification
  - Standard rack effects of the 1980s (chorus, delay, reverb)
- They were the first American Christian group to have a music video on MTV (for "Communication")
- Dana Key handled lead guitar and majority of lead vocals

#### Influence
- Alongside Petra, DeGarmo & Key proved Christian rock could compete with mainstream rock
- Their 1980s production values brought high-quality guitar tones to Christian music
- Influenced the generation of church guitarists who would later embrace the worship movement

## PRESET IMPLICATIONS

### How These Real-World Rigs Should Inform Helix Model Choices

#### Amp Models (Priority Order)
1. **Vox AC30 (Top Boost)** — "UK 2x12 Top Boost" in Helix. This is THE worship amp. Set clean to edge-of-breakup with master volume low and channel volume pushing tubes.
2. **Vox AC15** — "UK 1x12 Top Boost" in Helix. For recordings and smaller-room tones. Slightly smoother than AC30.
3. **Matchless DC-30** — "Matchstick" models in Helix. Richer, more articulate cleans than stock Vox models.
4. **Fender Deluxe Reverb** — "US Deluxe" models. Clean platform for drive pedals.
5. **Mesa Mark IV / V** — for Christian rock tones (Petra, Stryper). Different preset category entirely.
6. **Marshall JCM800** — for classic Christian rock. Not modern worship.

#### Cab/IR Choices
- **Vox Blue Alnico speakers** — the #1 worship speaker. Jensen Blue Alnicos specifically for Edge tones.
- **Celestion Greenback** — alternative for slightly warmer Vox tones
- **Celestion Vintage 30** — for heavier/rock presets

#### Signal Chain Order in Helix
Based on real-world rigs, the standard worship preset signal chain should be:
1. Volume Pedal (or input gain control)
2. Compressor (LA Studio Comp or Deluxe Comp)
3. Boost/Preamp block (Minotaur or Kinky Boost set clean)
4. Overdrive 1 — low gain (Scream 808 / Teemah)
5. Overdrive 2 — medium gain (Stupor OD / KWB)
6. Amp + Cab block
7. Modulation (if used — Chorus, Tremolo)
8. Delay 1 — analog character (Elephant Man = Memory Man model)
9. Delay 2 — digital dotted-eighth (Transistor Tape or Digital Delay)
10. Reverb (Glitz = shimmer, Plateaux = plate, Searchlights = hall)

#### Key Parameter Settings
- **Delay time**: Dotted-eighth note synced to tempo is THE worship delay. For 120 BPM = ~375ms. Always use tap tempo.
- **Delay feedback**: 3-5 repeats typical. Not infinite, not just one repeat.
- **Delay high-cut**: Roll off above 4-6kHz to darken repeats (per Edge's SDD-3000 settings)
- **Delay low-cut**: Roll off below 200-250Hz to prevent mud (per Edge's settings)
- **Delay modulation**: Subtle — rate ~5Hz, depth just until repeats slightly wobble
- **Reverb mix**: 20-35% for normal playing, 50%+ for ambient swells
- **Reverb decay**: 2-4 seconds for hall, shorter for plate
- **Drive level**: Low to medium. Worship overdrive is transparent, not saturated.
- **Compressor**: Gentle — 2:1 to 4:1 ratio, slow attack, medium release. Adds sustain without squashing dynamics.
- **Boost/preamp**: Always on. +3 to +6dB clean boost pushing the amp model slightly harder.

#### Pickup / Guitar Model Considerations
- Filter'Tron humbuckers (Gretsch) and single coils (Telecaster) dominate modern worship
- Helix presets should be voiced for bright, chimey pickups — NOT dark humbuckers
- If using a Les Paul or humbucker guitar, the amp EQ needs to compensate with more treble/presence
- Volume knob responsiveness is critical — worship guitarists use guitar volume constantly for dynamics

#### Preset Categories Based on This Research
1. **Modern Worship (Hillsong/Bethel/Elevation)** — AC30 + dotted-eighth delay + shimmer reverb + transparent OD
2. **UK Worship (Delirious? era)** — AC30 or Marshall + Kilt/Expandora drive + H9-style effects
3. **Jesus Culture** — AC30 + Memory Man analog delay + fuzz option + POG octave
4. **Ambient/Pad** — AC15 + stacked reverbs + reverse delay + volume swells (Nigel's "6 pedals at once" approach)
5. **Classic Christian Rock (Petra/Stryper)** — Mesa Mark or Marshall + higher gain + chorus
6. **Edge/U2 Worship** — AC30 + dotted-eighth delay (SDD-3000 character) + minimal drive + explorer/strat voicing
7. **Acoustic-Driven Worship (Maverick City)** — Warmer amp, less delay, more natural reverb, R&B-influenced dynamics

#### Critical Insight
The single most important element across ALL worship guitar rigs is **dynamics**. Every major worship guitarist emphasizes that dynamics — controlled by pick attack, guitar volume knob, and playing intensity — matter more than any pedal or amp setting. Helix presets should be designed with high touch-sensitivity and wide dynamic range. The preset should clean up dramatically when you back off the guitar volume and bloom when you dig in.
