## Background & Career Trajectory

### The Prodigy

Lincoln Brewster (born July 30, 1971) was essentially born into music. By age two, he was keeping time with broken drumsticks at his mom's band rehearsals. By five, he was playing mandolin for tourists in an Alaskan port town. By nine, he had picked up the electric guitar and never looked back. He grew up honing serious chops, developing the kind of technical facility that would put him in rarefied air among guitarists of any genre.

### The Steve Perry Connection

This is one of the most remarkable origin stories in worship music. In the early 1990s, Brewster was a young guitarist making instrumental demo recordings in the vein of players like Joe Satriani and Steve Vai. He gave a copy of his demo to Randy Jackson (yes, the future American Idol judge, who was then VP of A&R at Columbia Records). Jackson passed the tape to Steve Perry, the legendary vocalist of Journey.

Perry called Brewster at home and told him he loved his playing. At just 19 years old, Brewster was invited to audition as lead guitarist for Perry's solo project "For the Love of Strange Medicine" (1994). He got the gig. His guitar technique, tone, and equipment choices were noted for possessing qualities similar to Journey's Neal Schon -- bright, singing lead tones with classic rock muscle -- which was considered a favorable feature for Perry's sound.

Brewster toured with Perry for approximately six months from 1994 into early 1995. This was a legitimate mainstream rock career launching pad. He was playing arenas, working with one of the greatest rock vocalists in history, and learning what professional-tier musicianship looked like from the inside.

**What he learned from the Steve Perry era:**
- Professional-level preparation and execution standards
- How a great guitarist serves a vocalist and a song without disappearing
- Arena-level tone craft -- how to cut through a full band mix
- The Neal Schon school of melodic rock lead playing: singing lines, not just shred runs
- The difference between being a great player and being a professional musician

### The Conversion and Career Turn

Crucially, Brewster had accepted Christ before beginning work with Perry. After the tour, he faced a fork in the road: continue pursuing mainstream rock stardom or follow his faith. He chose to walk away from the secular music industry entirely.

He took a job at his local church -- a decision that, from a career standpoint, looked like career suicide. A guy who had just toured with Steve Perry was now running sound or playing guitar at a small church. But that decision set the trajectory for everything that followed.

### Ministry and Recording Career

That church job led to worship leading, which led to recognition within the Christian music community. Key milestones:

- Performed with Michael W. Smith during Smith's 1998 "Live the Life" tour
- Signed a recording deal with Integrity Music
- Released his self-titled debut album in 1999
- Rose to become Senior Worship Pastor at Bayside Church in Granite Bay, California (near Sacramento), one of the largest churches in the region
- Has been re-signed by Integrity Music multiple times, demonstrating sustained relevance

### Philosophy: When to Shred vs. When to Serve

Brewster's approach to the shred-vs-worship tension is essentially this: he doesn't see them as contradictory. His philosophy draws from Psalm 51 -- being genuine and real before God. He believes worship should be authentic, not "churchy or phoney."

His playing philosophy emphasizes that being a professional guitarist means acting professionally -- doing what's best for the setting you're in. This means:

- When the song calls for restraint, he plays with restraint
- When there's an instrumental break, he can absolutely let loose with blazing solos
- The solos serve the song's emotional arc rather than existing as showcases
- He sees his guitar pyrotechnics as another form of worship expression, not performance

As one reviewer noted, his solos "impress the listener but don't detract from the song -- they are part of the worship experience." This seamless integration of technical excellence into worship is his signature contribution to the genre.

## Gear -- Exhaustive Breakdown

### Guitars

**Primary Instruments: Fender Stratocasters**

Brewster is fundamentally a Strat player. This is significant -- he chose the bright, articulate voice of a Stratocaster over humbuckers for worship, giving him a more cutting, defined tone than many worship guitarists.

**Original Workhorse Guitars:**
- Two 1957-specification reissue Fender Stratocasters with DiMarzio pickups
  - One with Aztec Gold finish, ash body, maple fingerboard and neck
  - One with Olympic White/Pearl finish, ash body, maple fingerboard and neck
- Both guitars were eventually stolen, leading him to buy a Fender Eric Johnson Signature Stratocaster off the shelf on his way to a gig as a replacement

**The Fender Lincoln Brewster Signature Stratocaster:**

Brewster now has his own Fender signature model (2024 Artist Signature Series), which tells you everything about his status in the guitar world. Key specs:

- **Body:** Ash (tonewood choice matters -- ash has more bite and clarity than alder)
- **Finish:** Olympic Pearl (newer version) or Aztec Gold (original version)
- **Neck:** Maple with Soft "V" shape profile
- **Fingerboard:** Maple, 22 medium-jumbo frets
- **Radius:** 9.5" to 14" compound radius (flatter near the upper frets for easier bending and soloing)
- **Scale Length:** 25.5"
- **Pickups:** DiMarzio Area 58 (neck and middle) and DiMarzio Area 61 (bridge) -- these are noiseless single-coils that deliver vintage Strat tone without 60-cycle hum. This is a critical choice for worship environments where clean tones and quiet stages matter.
- **Active Boost:** Push/pull volume control activates an onboard active boost circuit
- **Mid-Boost:** Custom mid-boost controlled via Tone 1 knob -- this is a "stealth mod" that lets him push mids for lead work without switching guitars
- **Tremolo:** 2-Point Synchronized Tremolo with bent steel saddles and pop-in tremolo arm
- **Tuners:** Vintage-style locking tuners
- **Price:** $2,499

He describes it as a '57 Stratocaster with "stealth mods" -- modern appointments that update its sound and feel while maintaining that late-50s aesthetic.

**Other Guitars in the Collection:**
- A few Gibson Les Pauls
- A Gibson SG
- Various other guitars
- Borrowed a friend's McPherson classical guitar for recording (was impressed with it)
- Notable: No significant PRS usage documented despite his rock background

### Amplifiers -- The Evolution

**Early Career / Tube Era:**
- Marshall Plexi amplifiers (the real deal)
- His core sound has always been rooted in the Marshall Plexi voice -- bright, aggressive, British crunch
- He profiled his Marshall Plexi into a Kemper Profiler for recording flexibility

**The Digital Transition:**

Brewster was one of the earliest high-profile worship guitarists to embrace digital modeling, which makes him a pioneer in the worship-tech space. His amplification evolution:

1. **Real Marshall Plexi era** -- early career, traditional tube amps
2. **Line 6 POD X3 Live** -- adopted in the late 2000s as his primary live tone source
3. **Line 6 POD HD500 / HD500X** -- became his primary live rig for years. He went fully direct to PA with the floor unit, occasionally using a provided backline amp purely for stage monitoring volume
4. **Kemper Profiler** -- used significantly in studio recording, profiled his own Marshall Plexi
5. **Line 6 Helix / Helix Native** -- his current platform. Praised it as having "taken amp modeling, processing and user friendliness to a whole new level"

**The Core Amp Sound -- The Plexi Variac:**

Regardless of platform, the heart of Lincoln Brewster's tone is the same amp model:

**A 1968 Marshall Plexi Super Lead run through a variable AC (variac) transformer**

This is his foundational sound across all platforms. On Line 6 devices, this translates to the "Plexi Variac" amp model. Key characteristics:

- A variac-fed Plexi has slightly less headroom and a "saggier," more touch-responsive feel than a standard Plexi
- It breaks up a bit earlier and has a smoother, more compressed top end
- It responds beautifully to pick dynamics -- clean up with a lighter touch, crunch with a dig

**Amp Settings (The Brewster Sweet Spot):**
- **Drive:** Below half (he's not going for heavy saturation)
- **Bass:** Below half, kept tamed. Only increases slightly when cleaning up the sound
- **Mids:** Upper portion of the range (significant mid presence)
- **Treble:** Upper portion of the range
- **Presence:** Upper portion of the range

This creates a bright, mid-forward, articulate tone that cuts through a band mix without being muddy or boomy. The low bass setting is critical -- it prevents the low end from getting flubby in a church PA context.

**The "Brewster EQ Curve":**
- Boosted mids
- Rolled-off high-frequency shelf
- He applies this EQ shape frequently to smooth out the Plexi model
- This tames the ice-pick highs that a bright Strat through a Plexi can produce, while keeping the mid-forward cut

**Cabinet/Speaker Model:**
- 4x12 Marshall cabinet with Celestion Greenback 25 watt speakers
- Mic'd with a Shure SM-57 positioned on-axis

### Tonal Evolution Across Career

**Early period (1999-2005):** More raw rock energy, closer to the Steve Perry-era guitar hero sound. Heavier gain, more overt shredding. Real amps in the studio.

**Middle period (2006-2012):** Found the sweet spot between rock guitar and congregational worship. "Everlasting God" and "Today Is the Day" represent the peak of this era. Transition to digital modeling live while potentially still using real amps in studio.

**Later period (2014-present):** More refined, mature tone. Still guitar-forward but with better integration of technology. Helix Native and Kemper become the recording tools. The tone is smoother, more polished, but still unmistakably "Lincoln Brewster."

### Live vs. Studio Sound

- **Live:** Goes entirely direct to PA through his Line 6 modeler (HD500X or Helix). Sometimes uses a backline amp purely for stage monitoring/feel, but the PA sound comes from the modeler. This means his live tone is remarkably consistent night to night.
- **Studio:** Uses Helix Native plugin and Kemper Profiler with his own Marshall Plexi profiles. Records both a direct signal and a processed signal simultaneously, giving him re-amping flexibility in the mix. This dual-capture approach gives him the best of both worlds.
- **The gap is smaller than most artists** because he's using modeling in both contexts. His live and studio sounds are closer to each other than most guitarists achieve.

## Playing Style

### Rock/Shred Background Informing Worship

Brewster came up playing instrumental rock guitar in the vein of Satriani and Vai. His demo tape that caught Randy Jackson's attention was instrumental shred material. This means:

- He has legitimate alternate picking speed and accuracy
- His vibrato is wide and controlled (not the nervous wobble of a less experienced player)
- He understands modal interchange and scalar vocabulary beyond pentatonic boxes
- His bending accuracy is professional -- every bend hits the target note precisely
- He has a sophisticated understanding of harmony that lets him navigate chord changes fluidly

### Dynamics and Restraint

This is perhaps his most important contribution to worship guitar culture. Brewster demonstrated that a player can have elite chops AND exercise restraint:

- **Verse/Worship sections:** Clean to lightly overdriven, rhythmic support. Arpeggiated chords, tasteful fills. He becomes part of the band texture.
- **Chorus/Anthemic sections:** Pushes into moderate overdrive, bigger chord voicings, more energy. Still serving the song.
- **Instrumental breaks/Solos:** This is where he opens up. Confident, melodic solos with rock DNA -- string bends for vocal-like expressiveness, artificial harmonics for shimmering accents, delay for atmosphere.
- **He uses pick dynamics extensively:** The Plexi Variac setup responds beautifully to pick attack. Lighter touch = cleaner. Digging in = natural crunch. This is why his amp drive is set below half -- he wants that dynamic range.

### Rhythm Playing

His rhythm playing is often overlooked because the solos get the attention, but it's exceptional:

- Clean, articulate chord voicings -- you can hear every note, nothing is muddy
- Tight rhythmic feel from years of professional playing
- Uses open-string voicings and partial chords effectively for worship contexts
- Understands how to comp behind a vocal without stepping on it
- His rhythm tone is bright and defined, cutting through the mix without volume

### Lead Playing in Worship

What distinguishes Brewster's lead work from gratuitous shredding:

- **Melodic first:** His solos sing. The Neal Schon influence is clear -- singing, vocal-like melodic lines
- **Emotionally appropriate:** The solo matches the song's emotional arc
- **Technically impressive but not show-offy:** He uses his chops in service of expression
- **Rock vocabulary in worship context:** Pentatonic-based with modal color, confident bends, legato runs where appropriate
- **Sustain and tone quality:** His notes ring and sustain beautifully -- the Strat/Plexi combination gives him that singing quality

### Effects Usage -- Restrained Compared to Ambient Worship

Brewster's approach to effects is notably different from the Hillsong/Bethel ambient school:

- **No massive ambient reverb washes** -- his reverb is present but controlled
- **Delay is used for texture and sustain**, not the dotted-eighth rhythmic delay that defines the Hillsong sound
- **No heavy modulation effects** dominating his core sound
- **The overdrive is the primary "effect"** -- his tone shaping comes from gain staging and EQ, not from stacking ambient effects
- **Boss-pedal practical** -- his effects choices (BD-2, DD-3, RV-5) are utilitarian and reliable, not boutique or esoteric
- **The guitar tone itself is the statement**, not the effects chain wrapped around it

## Influence

### Proving Technical Excellence and Worship Are Not Contradictory

Before Brewster (and a handful of others), there was an unspoken ceiling on how good a worship guitarist was "supposed" to be. Too much flash was considered unspiritual or distracting. Brewster demolished that ceiling:

- He showed that a guitar solo could be an act of worship, not a performance ego trip
- His success proved that congregations respond to excellent musicianship
- He modeled how to integrate virtuosity without making the worship experience about the guitarist
- His pastoral role (he's a senior pastor, not just a hired musician) gave him credibility to push the boundaries

### Influence on a Generation of Worship Guitarists

Brewster inspired a specific type of worship guitarist: the player who wanted to actually PLAY, not just strum open chords with a capo:

- Gave permission for worship guitarists to practice and develop real chops
- Showed that you could use a Stratocaster (not just an acoustic) as your primary worship instrument
- Demonstrated that electric guitar could lead worship, not just support it
- His POD/Helix patches becoming community fixtures meant thousands of guitarists were literally starting from his tone as a template

### How He Differs from the Hillsong/Bethel Ambient School

This is a critical distinction for understanding worship guitar approaches:

| Aspect | Lincoln Brewster | Hillsong/Bethel Ambient |
|--------|-----------------|------------------------|
| **Core tone** | Strat through Plexi (rock) | Humbucker or Strat through Vox/Fender (ambient) |
| **Effects philosophy** | Effects support the guitar tone | Effects ARE the tone |
| **Delay approach** | Standard delay for depth | Dotted-eighth rhythmic delay as signature |
| **Reverb** | Moderate, controlled | Large, atmospheric, defining |
| **Gain** | Plexi crunch, natural breakup | Lighter gain, more headroom for effects |
| **Lead playing** | Melodic rock solos, confident | Ambient swells, textural |
| **Rhythm approach** | Driving rock rhythms | Arpeggiated, atmospheric patterns |
| **Dynamic approach** | Pick dynamics, volume knob | Effects switching, volume swells |
| **Overall vibe** | Rock band playing worship | Atmospheric soundscape |

## The Steve Perry Connection -- Deeper Implications

### What the Professional Rock World Taught Him

The Steve Perry experience wasn't just a cool story -- it fundamentally shaped his approach:

1. **Standards of excellence:** Playing for Steve Perry meant every note mattered. This translated directly to treating worship with the same professional seriousness.

2. **Tone consciousness:** Working alongside the legacy of Neal Schon meant understanding that guitar tone is not an afterthought -- it's essential to the musical experience.

3. **Serving the song and vocalist:** In Perry's band, the guitar served Perry's voice. Brewster learned that a great guitarist makes everyone around them sound better. This is exactly what worship demands.

4. **Arena-scale thinking:** Understanding how guitar sits in a large-venue mix informed his later approach to church sound, where the same principles apply.

5. **The courage to walk away:** Perhaps most importantly, having tasted mainstream success and choosing to leave it for ministry gave him a credibility and freedom that a purely church-background musician wouldn't have. He didn't serve worship because he couldn't make it in rock -- he served worship because he chose to.

## Cultural Impact

### Legitimizing Electric Guitar Excellence in Worship

Brewster's career represents a paradigm shift in how churches view electric guitar:

- **Before Brewster's era:** Electric guitar in worship was often tolerated, sometimes controversial. "Too loud," "too worldly," "too distracting" were common criticisms.
- **After Brewster (and contemporaries):** Electric guitar became an accepted, even celebrated, element of modern worship. Churches actively sought guitarists who could play well.

### The Performance vs. Worship Tension

Brewster navigated this tension through several strategies:

1. **Pastoral authority:** As a senior pastor, he has spiritual credibility. His guitar playing is clearly coming from a place of worship leadership, not performance ambition.
2. **Authenticity:** His emphasis on being "genuine" (citing Psalm 51) frames his playing as authentic expression rather than showing off.
3. **Integration:** His solos are woven into the worship experience rather than bolted on. They build the emotional arc of the song.
4. **Humility in interviews:** He consistently frames his guitar ability as a gift to be used in service, not a platform for ego.
5. **The testimony:** His story of walking away from Steve Perry and mainstream rock to serve the church is powerful evidence that his priorities are spiritual, not performative.

### How Churches Received Him

The reception was overwhelmingly positive because:

- Congregations could feel the difference between a skilled player serving the song and a mediocre player struggling through it
- His songs are genuinely singable -- they work for congregational worship despite the guitar sophistication
- The energy and excellence he brought attracted people (especially men and younger churchgoers) who might have found traditional worship music unengaging
- His technology adoption (going direct with modelers) showed churches that great guitar tone didn't require deafening amp volumes on stage

## PRESET IMPLICATIONS

What Lincoln Brewster's approach teaches us about worship preset design:

### 1. The Plexi Variac Is the Foundation
His entire career is built on one amp model: a variac-fed 1968 Marshall Plexi Super Lead. Any "Brewster-style" preset starts here. This is NOT the same amp approach as the Hillsong/ambient school (which leans Vox AC30 or Fender clean platforms). The Plexi Variac gives:
- More natural compression and sag
- Touch-sensitive breakup
- A fundamentally "rock" character that still cleans up beautifully

### 2. The EQ Profile Is Distinctive
- Mids boosted (the "Brewster EQ curve")
- High shelf rolled off (taming Strat brightness through a Plexi)
- Bass kept lean and controlled
- This is a mid-forward, articulate voice designed to cut through a full band without being harsh

### 3. Gain Is Lower Than You'd Think
- Drive below 50% on the amp
- He relies on pick dynamics to control the gain, not the amp setting
- The preset should clean up dramatically when you back off your pick attack
- This is NOT a high-gain metal tone -- it's moderate-gain classic rock territory

### 4. Greenback Cabinet Is Essential
- 4x12 Marshall with Celestion Greenback 25s
- SM-57 on-axis
- Greenbacks have a mid-forward character with controlled top end that complements the Plexi

### 5. Effects Are Supporting Cast, Not the Star
- Delay: Standard digital delay for depth, NOT dotted-eighth rhythmic delay
- Reverb: Present but controlled, NOT the massive ambient washes of Hillsong-style worship
- Overdrive: A Boss BD-2 or similar transparent boost for pushing solos, not tone-reshaping distortion
- The effects enhance the guitar-amp tone rather than replacing it

### 6. This Represents the "Rock Worship" School
Brewster-style presets serve a different congregation than ambient worship presets:
- Guitarists who want to actually play lead lines and solos
- Churches with a more rock-forward worship style
- Players coming from a rock/blues background entering worship
- Situations where the guitar is the lead instrument, not a texture layer

### 7. Dynamic Range Is the Secret Weapon
The preset must respond to pick dynamics. The difference between a gentle strum and a hard pick attack should be dramatic -- from glassy clean to gritty crunch. This is where the Plexi Variac model shines and why his gain is set conservatively.

### 8. Single-Coil Optimized
His tone is fundamentally shaped by single-coil Stratocaster pickups (specifically noiseless DiMarzio Area models). The presets should be voiced for single-coil input -- brighter, more articulate, with the EQ compensating for any harshness rather than trying to simulate humbucker warmth.

### 9. The "Direct to PA" Mindset
Brewster has been going direct with modelers for nearly two decades. His presets are designed to sound good through a PA system, not necessarily through a guitar cab on stage. This means:
- The cab simulation and mic modeling are critical components, not afterthoughts
- The tone is full-range (not the band-limited sound of a guitar cab in a room)
- What you hear in the modeler IS the final tone

### 10. Worship Presets Need Multiple Gain Stages
Following Brewster's dynamic approach, a worship preset suite should include:
- **Clean:** Glassy Strat sparkle through a barely-breaking-up Plexi (volume knob rolled back)
- **Rhythm crunch:** Moderate drive for verse/chorus rhythm work
- **Lead boost:** BD-2 style transparent boost pushing the Plexi into singing sustain territory
- All three should feel like the SAME amp at different gain stages, not three different sounds

---

*Lincoln Brewster represents the rock-worship school of guitar -- the antithesis and perfect complement to the ambient/textural approach. Where Hillsong players build soundscapes, Brewster builds rock anthems. Both are legitimate worship approaches, and a complete worship preset library needs to serve both traditions.*
