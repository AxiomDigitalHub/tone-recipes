# Lesser-Known Worship Guitar: Scenes, Movements, and Experiences Beyond the Mainstream

*Research compiled for faderandknob.com -- April 2026*

---

## Underground and Indie Worship Guitar

### The Liturgists / Michael Gungor

The Liturgists began in 2014 as a collaboration between Michael Gungor and Mike McHargue (Science Mike), self-described as "an experimental art collective." Their work sits at the intersection of science, art, and faith, attracting a remarkably diverse audience including agnostics and atheists -- over 250,000 subscribers who feel marginalized by mainstream Christianity.

**Guitar approach:** Gungor studied jazz guitar at Western Michigan University and University of North Texas. His sound draws from indie-folk instrumentation reminiscent of Sufjan Stevens combined with ambient soundscapes. His pedalboard reflects this: JHS Pulp 'N' Peel compressor, JHS Morning Glory overdrive, TMG DRHIVE, JHS Panther Cub delay, and Strymon blueSky reverb. Guitar of choice includes the Fender Telecaster Thinline.

**Why this matters for Fader & Knob:** This is a worship guitarist who studied jazz, plays through boutique pedals, and creates atmospheric, textural music that doesn't fit the Bethel/Elevation template at all. His audience is the "spiritually curious but institutionally skeptical" crowd. There's a whole segment of worship guitarists who want sounds that evoke wonder without sounding like a megachurch.

### Gungor (the band)

Michael Gungor and Lisa Gungor created music described as "alternative, folk, textured and experimental." The band blends folk, indie, and electronic music with expansive arrangements. This is worship music that could sit on the same shelf as Bon Iver or Radiohead.

**Gear notes:** Fender Telecaster Thinline, JHS pedals throughout, Strymon reverb. The sound is built on layers and texture rather than the standard worship delay-wash approach.

### Josh Garrels

A true indie model -- self-records, mixes, produces, and distributes without label or management since 2002. His sound ranges from pastoral indie folk to hip-hop influenced production. He has resisted being labeled as strictly "Christian" or "secular," letting the music speak for itself.

**Guitar aesthetic:** Remarkable fingerpicking and acoustic work. Lo-fi bedroom recordings characterize his early work. The production is intimate and handmade. No chase for polish.

**Why this matters:** Garrels represents the worship musician who doesn't want to sound like a worship musician. His audience doesn't shop at the same stores as the Hillsong crowd. They want authenticity over production value.

### John Mark McMillan

Known for "How He Loves" -- one of the most covered worship songs of the last 20 years. But McMillan's own performances are raw, rock-forward, and loud. He performs at volumes suited for rock clubs, not sanctuaries.

**Gear:** Elliott Guitars, JHS Pedals. Studio work has included a Trainwreck Circuits Rocket amp (a rare, coveted boutique amp). His endorsement of JHS and use of boutique/vintage gear shows a guitarist who prioritizes character over convenience.

**Key insight:** McMillan left touring Christian music. The evolution of Christian music shifted from guitar-driven rock of the 2000s to arena-rock anthems and pop production. Guitarists like McMillan who want raw, aggressive worship tones are underserved by the current ecosystem.

### Housefires

Founded at Grace Midtown Church in Atlanta by Pat Barrett, Kirby Kaple, and Nate Moore. Created "Build My Life" -- one of the most sung worship songs globally. Their recordings are live, raw, and often captured in home and church settings.

**Guitar sound:** Minimal instrumentation. Acoustic-forward. The philosophy is that worship should be approachable without barriers of heavy production or performance. Their monthly worship nights at Grace Midtown have an organic, in-the-room quality.

**Why this matters:** Housefires proves you can write world-shaping worship songs without a massive production rig. This is a model for the 90% of worship guitarists who play in rooms under 300 people. Their sound is achievable.

### Sandra McCracken

GRAMMY-nominated, Dove Award-winning. Blends folk, gospel, and hymn traditions. Her Psalm settings draw from artists as diverse as U2, Bela Fleck, and Rich Mullins. Co-founded Rain for Roots (children's music collective) with Ellie Holcomb.

**Guitar approach:** Emotive, Americana-influenced. Acoustic-driven, storytelling-focused. This is worship music that sounds like it belongs on a front porch in Nashville.

### Ellie Holcomb

Nashville-based singer-songwriter. Her work points toward trust and faith through personal experience. Part of the Rain for Roots collective with McCracken.

**Guitar approach:** Simple, acoustic, singer-songwriter style. The guitar serves the vocal and the lyric, not the other way around.

### Citizens (formerly Citizens & Saints)

Formed at Mars Hill Church U-District in Seattle, led by Zach Bolen. Their sound drew comparisons to Passion Pit and Two Door Cinema Club -- club rock grooves with gospel-centric lyrics. Indie rock with electronic beats, shimmering with the emotional intensity of worship.

**Guitar sound:** High-energy indie rock. Electronic textures layered with guitar. Their second album incorporated more synthesizers. This was worship music that could play at an indie rock venue and not feel out of place.

**Key insight:** Citizens represent a brief moment where indie rock aesthetics fully merged with worship music. The Mars Hill dissolution scattered these musicians, but the sonic template they created still influences worship teams that want to sound less generic.

### Kings Kaleidoscope

Led by Chad Gardner, formed at a Mars Hill Church plant at the University of Washington in 2010. Features a large rotating cast of collaborators.

**Musical DNA:** Gardner grew up as a hip-hop DJ, which explains why the band has two drummers. The sound is described as "indie rock meets hip hop production with a sprinkle of Disney," with influences from math rock, hip-hop, and Broken Social Scene. One reviewer called it "experimental Jazz with a freedom to be and go wherever it feels like it should go."

**Guitar role:** Chad Gardner plays guitar and sings, but the guitar sits within a dense, eclectic arrangement that includes woodwinds, brass, and strings. Guitar is one color in a large palette, not the dominant voice.

**Why this matters:** Kings Kaleidoscope shows that worship guitar doesn't have to be the ambient pad machine. It can be rhythmically complex, jazz-influenced, and part of a larger textural conversation.

### The Brilliance

Duo of David Gungor (Michael's brother) and John Arndt, formed in 2010 in Marshfield, Wisconsin. Their sound blends classical aesthetic with traditional pop form, built on piano and string arrangements.

**Guitar use:** Guitar is not the primary instrument. Piano and strings dominate. When guitar appears, it serves the arrangement rather than leading it. This is worship music where the guitar knows when to be absent.

**Philosophy:** They believe art's beauty is its ability to transcend clean definitions. Their music gives listeners "time and space to fully soak in the beauty" -- a stark contrast to the energy-driven megachurch model.

## Specific Church Experiences and Contexts

### The Small Church Reality (100-200 people)

This is the MAJORITY of churches and the majority of our potential customers.

**The setup:** Often one guitarist (acoustic or electric, sometimes both roles on one person). No sound engineer -- or a volunteer with minimal training running sound. The room is a multipurpose space, not a purpose-built auditorium. Budget is minimal.

**Key challenges:**
- Getting acceptable tone in rooms with terrible acoustics (tile floors, parallel walls, low ceilings)
- Competing with a PA system that may be underpowered or poorly EQ'd
- No in-ear monitors -- playing off floor wedges or nothing
- Equipment complexity must match volunteer skill level
- Gear purchased one pedal at a time over months or years

**The human element:** Plan for your volunteers, not audio professionals. Sophisticated features you can't use effectively are wasted investment. Excellence isn't about the most expensive equipment -- it's about the right tools for your context.

### House Church / Small Group Worship

**The environment:** Living rooms, basements, patios. 10-30 people. No PA system. No stage.

**What works:**
- Parlor-size acoustic guitars with intimate tone suited for quieter settings
- If electric, clean tone with reverb and perhaps a touch of chorus, amp volume way down
- The "Acoustic+ Movement" concept: electronic aids that enhance but don't overtake the acoustic nature of the space
- Songs that sound complete with just a guitar and voices

**Key insight:** A complete worship arc can be created with nothing more than an acoustic guitar and willing voices. This is where worship began and where much of it still happens.

### Outdoor Worship (Festivals, Camps, Mission Trips)

**Acoustic challenges:** No walls for sound to bounce off. Competing with wind, traffic, nature. Often no power or limited power. Gear gets exposed to weather, dust, humidity.

**What works:** Campfire worship means no access to amplifiers or a full band. Songs need to sound great with just a guitar. Acoustic-friendly worship songs that allow everyone to join in easily.

**Gear considerations:** Rugged, portable, battery-powered where possible. A modeler like Helix with headphones could be a practice tool, but the actual performance context is usually purely acoustic.

### Prison Ministry Worship

**Equipment restrictions are severe:**
- Federal Bureau of Prisons prohibits prisoners from using or possessing electric guitars or electronic instruments
- Exception: electric guitars owned by the Religious Services Department, used exclusively for worship
- Volunteers cannot bring computers, sound equipment, or electronic devices without prior approval
- In UK prisons, acoustic guitars with nylon strings only (steel strings can be weaponized for tattoo needles; HIV/Hep C risk)
- Policies vary by prison level, state, and warden discretion

**The reality:** Prison worship guitarists play nylon-string acoustics in reverberant concrete rooms with no effects, no amplification, and often instruments in poor condition. And yet, some of the most powerful worship experiences happen in exactly these settings.

**Why this matters for Fader & Knob:** This is the extreme end of "making it work with what you have." Understanding this context builds empathy for every guitarist who feels limited by their gear.

### The Village Church (Matt Chandler) and "Normal Church" Worship

Churches like The Village Church in Flower Mound, TX represent the large-but-not-megachurch segment. Gospel-centered, less focused on production spectacle than Elevation or Hillsong. The worship is congregational rather than performative.

**Guitar's role:** Supportive. Not trying to recreate a record. Serving the congregation's singing rather than putting on a show. This is where most competent church worship bands live -- skilled enough to be good, not resourced enough (or interested in) being a production machine.

## Genre-Bending Worship Guitar

### Worship Hip-Hop/Trap

**Key artists:** Lecrae, Andy Mineo, KB (all connected to Reach Records / 116 collective). Lecrae's "Let the Trap Say Amen" (2018, with producer Zaytoven) explicitly merged trap production with worship.

**Guitar's role:** Minimal and sample-based. In hip-hop/trap worship, guitar appears as chopped samples, lo-fi loops, or occasional clean arpeggios layered into beats. It's not a live-performance instrument in this context -- it's a production element. When guitar appears, it tends to be neo-soul clean tones or heavily processed textures.

**Insight:** This genre mostly bypasses live guitar entirely. But there's an interesting opportunity: worship teams at predominantly Black churches often blend hip-hop sensibilities with live instrumentation. The guitarist in that context needs R&B/neo-soul chops, not ambient worship pad skills.

### Worship R&B/Neo-Soul (Maverick City)

Maverick City Music, founded by Tony Brown and Jonathan Jay in Atlanta, blends gospel, contemporary worship, R&B, and spontaneous praise. Their live recordings include spontaneous prayer and exhortation -- they don't edit out emotion.

**Guitar's role:** Groove instrument, not atmosphere machine. Think clean tones with compression, rhythmic strumming and chord stabs, neo-soul chord voicings (major 7ths, 9ths, sus chords). The guitar locks in with bass and keys to create a pocket, rather than floating above the mix with delay trails.

**Why this matters:** Maverick City has opened a massive door for worship that doesn't sound white and suburban. The guitar skills needed here are fundamentally different from the Hillsong/Bethel template -- more James Brown than The Edge.

### Worship EDM

**Does it exist?** Yes. Hillsong Young & Free pioneered the approach with EDM and pop-influenced worship. The genre has grown to include artists featured on platforms dedicated to Christian EDM.

**Guitar's role in EDM worship:** Complementary to electronic elements. Characteristics include layers of programmed backing tracks, quarter-note bass thumps, and build/drop cycles at ~130 BPM. Guitar plays power chords or three-note chord inversions. Even Hillsong will have an electric guitarist just playing chords most of the time in electronic songs. Guitar provides harmonic foundation and texture, not melodic dominance.

**Key insight:** In EDM worship, the guitarist needs to know when NOT to play. Space and restraint matter more than technique.

### Worship Metal

**The scene:** Christian metalcore is a significant subgenre. Key bands include For Today, August Burns Red, Underoath, The Devil Wears Prada, Silent Planet, Wolves at the Gate, Demon Hunter, and Fit for a King.

**Guitar approach:** August Burns Red features highly melodic guitar riffs with technical or odd time signatures and breakdowns. These bands are "Christians in a band" rather than "Christian bands" -- the distinction matters. The guitar work is technically demanding: drop tunings, palm muting, sweep picking, complex rhythmic patterns.

**Connection to worship:** These bands don't typically play Sunday morning services. But their members often attend churches and some lead worship in stripped-down settings. The tension between their artistic expression and Sunday morning accessibility is real.

### Worship Jazz

**Does it exist as a scene?** Yes, though it's niche. "Sacred jazz" has existed since the 1950s, combining Black gospel music and jazz. Many great jazz musicians grew up in the church and had their first musical experience in faith communities.

**Notable examples:** Rev. Bill Carter performs with his band Presbybop and has organized a yearly jazz communion at First Presbyterian Church of Clarks Summit, PA for over 23 years. Mary Lou Williams composed three Masses in the jazz idiom after converting to Catholicism. John Coltrane's "A Love Supreme" was offered as a musical prayer.

**Guitar in worship jazz:** Jazz guitar in worship settings uses clean tones, complex chord voicings (extended harmonies, altered dominants), comping patterns, and improvisational passages. A jazz worship guitarist needs fundamentally different skills than a contemporary worship guitarist -- chord-melody technique, walking bass lines on guitar, and the ability to improvise over changes.

### Worship Bluegrass/Folk (Appalachian Traditions)

**Deep roots:** Appalachian music derives from ballads, hymns, and fiddle music of the British Isles. Bill Monroe described bluegrass as "a part of Methodist, Holiness and Baptist traditions. It's blues and jazz, and it has a high lonesome sound."

**Guitar's traditional role:** In bluegrass, the guitar rarely takes the lead, acting as a rhythm instrument. One exception: gospel-based songs, where guitar sometimes steps forward. The standard bluegrass guitar approach is boom-chuck rhythm with occasional flatpicked runs.

**Church context:** Appalachian life was deeply tied to religion. Scots-Irish Presbyterian and Baptist hymns transitioned into the gospel harmony that became integral to bluegrass vocals. Sunday morning bluegrass gospel bands still exist throughout Appalachia, and learning those songs remains an oral tradition.

**Modern crossover:** Artists like Josh Garrels blend this folk/Appalachian sensibility with modern worship. There's a growing "folk hymn" movement that draws on these traditions.

### Worship Reggae

**Christafari** pioneered Christian reggae in the U.S., founded by Mark Mohr in 1989. They fuse roots, dancehall, and dub with Latin, soca, and rock influences.

**Guitar approach:** Classic reggae guitar -- offbeat "skank" rhythm, clean tone with slight compression, often using barre chords on the upper strings. In Christafari's context, guitar sits within an ensemble of vibrant horns, heavy bass, and rich harmonies. The guitar's role is rhythmic and textural, not melodic.

### Latin Worship

**Miel San Marcos** is one of the most transcendent Christian bands in contemporary Latin music, founded in 2000 by the Morales brothers in San Marcos, Guatemala. They've toured 25+ countries, won 4 Dove Awards, and earned a Latin GRAMMY nomination.

**Guitar sound:** Guitar-driven praise with soaring harmonies. Driving guitar riffs paired with potent drumming. Their production experiments with pop, rock, and EDM influences with electronic sounds, while staying rooted in praise and worship. The guitar tone is more aggressive and present than typical North American worship -- less ambient wash, more rock energy.

**Why this matters:** Latin worship is a massive, growing market in the U.S. Spanish-language worship teams need tones and presets too. Miel San Marcos' guitar-forward approach is distinct from the ambient Hillsong template.

## The Multi-Guitarist Problem

### How Churches Divide Sonic Space

When multiple guitarists play together, clashing parts and tones are the primary problem. The recommended division:

**The Four Modern Worship Guitar Roles:**
1. **Rhythm guitar** -- Foundational chords, often acoustic or clean/light-drive electric. Provides harmonic body.
2. **Lead guitar** -- Melodic lines, riffs, intros/outros. Higher register to avoid blending into rhythm.
3. **Pad/ambient guitar** -- Swells, volume knob work, heavy reverb/delay. Creates atmosphere.
4. **Texture/effects** -- Dotted-eighth delays, tremolo picking, unique sounds for specific moments.

**Key principles:**
- Both guitarists should never play leads simultaneously -- someone needs to give the song body with rhythm
- Chord instruments are most detrimental to band definition when everyone plays the same voicings in the same register
- Keep lead instruments in higher ranges to prevent blending with rhythm
- Playing everyone in the same mid-low range creates mud

### Tone Conflicts Between Guitarists

- Different gain levels create volume wars
- Competing delay times create rhythmic confusion
- Two guitarists both using heavy reverb creates an indistinct wash
- The solution is intentional frequency separation: one guitarist uses a Tele with light drive, the other uses a humbucker guitar with more gain, etc.

### Nashville Number System

The lingua franca of worship music. Replaces chord names with numbers based on the scale degree: 1-5-6m-4 instead of G-D-Em-C.

**Why it matters:** If the worship leader changes the key, the chart stays the same. If you learn the numbers for G and C formations, you can play every key with a capo. This system is nearly universal in worship settings, and any resource targeting worship guitarists needs to speak this language.

**Practical application:** Worship leaders communicate changes by saying "go to the 4" rather than "go to C." Musicians who memorize their I, IV, V, and vi chords for each key are most of the way there.

## The Volunteer Worship Guitarist: Understanding the Customer

### Who They Are

Most worship guitarists are unpaid volunteers with day jobs. They are not professional musicians. They play because they love their church and feel called to serve.

### Typical Gear Budget

- **Lifetime guitar gear spending:** Average guitar player spends ~$10,000 over a lifetime (Fender data)
- **Starter worship pedalboard:** $500 range (including board, power supply, cables, and 3-4 pedals)
- **Mid-range worship setup:** ~$700 pedalboard is the "realistic" alternative to $5,000 dream boards
- **Full rig under $2,000:** Guitar + amp/modeler + pedalboard -- this is the ceiling for most
- **The reality:** Pedalboards are purchased one pedal at a time over months or years. Musicians don't have thousands of dollars laying available at once.
- **Minimum pedalboard infrastructure:** Board ($100) + power supply ($100) + cables/velcro ($100) = $300 before a single effect pedal

### Practice Reality

- Get the setlist on Tuesday (sometimes Thursday)
- Play on Sunday
- Maybe one group rehearsal on Thursday or Saturday
- Personal practice time: 30-60 minutes per week at best
- They need to learn 4-6 songs, often with specific parts from the recording

### Skill Level

- Intermediate: can play barre chords, basic open chords, simple lead lines
- May not read music or understand theory deeply
- Nashville Number System is learned out of necessity
- Can follow a chord chart but may struggle with complex rhythmic notation
- Ear training is often underdeveloped -- they match sounds by trial and error

### What Frustrates Them Most

1. **Getting "that sound" from the recording** -- They hear the album version and can't figure out how to replicate it with their gear
2. **Feeling overwhelmed by music theory** and struggling to find worship-specific training
3. **Gear anxiety** -- believing the "right" gear will make them sound professional, when what they need is practice
4. **Team dynamics** -- unfriendliness, snobbery from other musicians, lack of bonding
5. **Role confusion** -- not knowing what to play when there are multiple guitarists
6. **Volume/tone battles** with the sound engineer and other musicians
7. **Limited time** -- they can't spend hours dialing in tones

### What They Actually Need

- Presets that get them 80% of the way to the record's tone in minutes, not hours
- Clear guidance on WHAT to play (parts/roles), not just HOW to play
- Solutions that work in their real room, not a recording studio
- Affordable tools that respect their budget
- Quick-start resources for weekly song prep

## INSIGHTS FOR FADER & KNOB

### 1. Our Customer Is Not a Professional Musician

The typical Fader & Knob customer is a volunteer who got the setlist Tuesday, has 30 minutes to practice, owns a $700 pedalboard built one pedal at a time, and needs to sound passable by Sunday. They don't want tweaking -- they want results. Presets that require extensive adjustment will frustrate them. Presets that work out of the box will earn loyalty.

### 2. The "Sound Like the Record" Problem Is Our Core Value Proposition

The #1 frustration is not being able to replicate what they hear on the album. This is literally the job-to-be-done for Fader & Knob. Every piece of content, every preset, every tutorial should orient around closing this gap.

### 3. Small Church Is the Majority Market

Most worship happens in rooms under 300 people with one guitarist, no sound engineer, and a modest PA. Presets optimized for massive auditoriums won't translate. We should test and optimize for small-room acoustics, modest monitoring, and situations where the guitarist IS the band.

### 4. There Are Underserved Genre Niches

- **Latin worship** (Miel San Marcos style) -- guitar-forward, more aggressive than ambient. Spanish-language worship is growing fast in the U.S.
- **R&B/neo-soul worship** (Maverick City style) -- groove-based, clean tones, jazz voicings. Fundamentally different from the Hillsong template.
- **Folk/acoustic worship** (Housefires, Josh Garrels style) -- intimate, minimal production. "Less is more" preset packs.
- **Indie rock worship** (Citizens, Kings Kaleidoscope style) -- for teams that want to sound less generic.

### 5. The Multi-Guitarist Problem Is a Content Opportunity

How to divide sonic space, avoid mud, and complement (not compete with) another guitarist -- this is daily pain for worship teams. Preset packs designed as complementary pairs (rhythm + lead, or clean + driven) could be unique in the market.

### 6. Context-Specific Presets Are Underexplored

Nobody is making presets for:
- House church / living room worship (quiet, intimate, no PA)
- Outdoor worship (bright, cutting, no room reflections)
- Small room with bad acoustics (compensating EQ)
- The "only guitarist" scenario (filling more sonic space)
- Latin worship tones
- Neo-soul/R&B worship grooves

### 7. The Nashville Number System Is the Common Language

Any educational content, blog posts, or tutorials should use the Nashville Number System. Our audience thinks in numbers, not note names. Speaking their language builds instant credibility.

### 8. The Helix Worship Community Is Active and Reachable

Worship Helix users already gather in Facebook groups, Line 6 forums, and around channels like Worship Tutorials. These are concentrated, reachable audiences. The community already buys premium patches -- the market is validated.

### 9. Budget Sensitivity Is Real

Our pricing needs to respect the reality that these are unpaid volunteers who buy gear one piece at a time. A $500 pedalboard is their entire guitar budget for the year. Preset pricing should feel like an obvious value, not a luxury.

### 10. The "Worship Guitar" Category Is Broader Than We Think

Worship guitar isn't just ambient pads and dotted-eighth delays. It's reggae skank, Appalachian flatpicking, jazz comping, neo-soul groove, metal breakdowns, folk fingerpicking, and EDM power chords. The wider we cast our net, the more underserved musicians we reach. But the core -- the volunteer playing Sunday morning in a 200-person church -- that's where we build the foundation.
