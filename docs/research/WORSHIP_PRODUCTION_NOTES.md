His recordings tend toward polished but organic -- real amps, real performances, with studio refinement layered on top.

**Guitar recording approach:** Cash's Nashville studio workflow follows the standard Nashville production chain: track foundational performances live with the band, then overdub and refine guitar parts. Chislett, as both producer and guitarist, has the unique advantage of understanding exactly which guitar textures to stack and where.

Jason Ingram is one of the most prolific worship songwriters and producers, co-writing "Great Are You Lord," "Build My Life," and dozens of other modern worship standards.

### Nigel Hendroff (Producer and Guitarist)

Nigel Hendroff is the Music Director and lead guitarist of Hillsong Church whose playing appears on every Hillsong Church album since *You Are My World*. Beyond performing, he serves as a producer and music director.

When producing, Hendroff approaches guitar as an environmental element -- it creates atmosphere and emotional context rather than just playing chords. This production philosophy means guitar tracks on Hillsong records serve specific roles: some provide rhythmic foundation, others create ambient beds, and others provide melodic hooks or textural ear candy.

## Studio Signal Routing

### How Worship Guitar Tracks Are Typically Recorded

Modern worship guitar recording uses multiple approaches, often combined:

1. **Miked amplifier (most common for featured parts):** SM57 on an AC30 or similar amp remains the standard for capturing the core worship guitar tone. The amp's natural breakup, speaker response, and room interaction are considered essential to the sound.

2. **DI + amp blend:** Many producers capture a clean DI simultaneously with the miked amp signal. This provides flexibility for re-amping later or blending a clean, direct signal with the amp tone for clarity in dense mixes.

3. **Re-amping:** Recording a clean DI first, then sending it back through amplifiers later. This allows the producer to audition different amp tones, mic positions, and gain levels after the performance is captured. Re-amping is particularly useful when layering multiple guitar textures from a single performance.

4. **Amp modeling / direct recording:** Increasingly common, especially for ambient and textural parts where the character comes more from effects than amp breakup. Kemper Profilers, Fractal Audio units, and Line 6 Helix systems capture tones that can be refined later.

### Typical Number of Guitar Tracks

Based on album credits and multitrack stem analysis:

- **Simple worship ballad (verse/chorus):** 3-5 guitar tracks (acoustic foundation, clean electric, ambient pad, lead line, textural swell)
- **Full worship anthem (building arrangement):** 6-10+ guitar tracks (doubled rhythm guitars panned L/R, acoustic strumming, ambient washes, lead melody, textural layers, swell elements)
- **Hillsong United / Hillsong Worship density:** Up to 6 different electric guitarists credited per album, suggesting 8-12+ guitar tracks on climactic songs

MultiTracks.com and similar services typically provide stems including: two electric guitar tracks (EG1 and EG2), acoustic guitar, and sometimes additional guitar elements grouped into "keys/pads" or "other" categories. This suggests the standard production template includes at minimum 3-4 distinct guitar voices.

### Panning Strategies

The stereo field in worship guitar production follows established rock/pop conventions:

- **Acoustic guitar:** Often panned slightly off-center (10-30% L or R) or doubled and panned hard left/right
- **Rhythm electric guitars:** Double-tracked and panned hard left (100%) and hard right (100%) -- the classic wall-of-sound technique. Recording the same part twice creates natural phase differences that widen the stereo image dramatically
- **Lead electric:** Panned center or slightly off-center to maintain focus
- **Ambient/textural guitars:** Wide stereo placement using stereo delay and reverb returns panned hard L/R
- **Pad/swell guitars:** Spread across the stereo field, often using stereo effects to fill the space between the hard-panned rhythm guitars

**The "wall of sound" effect:** Created by recording the same guitar part multiple times with slight performance variations (not copied tracks), then panning each take across the stereo field. Even two takes panned left and right create significant width. Adding a third take panned center, or additional takes at intermediate positions, increases density. The subtle timing and tonal differences between takes create a massive, enveloping sound.

### DI + Amp Blend Ratios

In worship production, the blend between DI and amp signals depends on the role:
- **Clean/ambient parts:** DI signal may be prominent (50-70% DI) for clarity, with amp adding warmth
- **Driven rhythm parts:** Amp signal dominant (80-90% amp) for natural character, DI for attack definition
- **Textural/effects-heavy parts:** Often recorded entirely through pedals into the DI, or with the amp signal used only for body

## Mic Techniques for Worship Guitar

### SM57 on AC30 (The Standard)

The Shure SM57 on a Vox AC30 is the foundational worship guitar recording setup:

- **On-axis, center of speaker cone:** Maximum brightness, midrange bite, and presence. This is the "default" position for aggressive, cutting tones.
- **Off-axis (angled 15-45 degrees):** Reduces harshness in the upper mids while retaining body. Many worship producers prefer this for cleaner tones that need to sit behind vocals.
- **Moving toward the speaker surround (edge):** Reduces midrange and upper-mid frequencies for a darker, warmer tone. Useful for rhythm parts that need to fill space without competing.
- **Distance:** Close-miking (1-3 inches) increases bass via proximity effect and reduces room sound. Pulling back to 6-12 inches captures more natural speaker response but picks up room ambience.
- **The "face test":** Experienced engineers put their ear (or face) directly in front of the speaker to hear what the mic will capture, then position accordingly. Moving even an inch changes the tone dramatically.

### Ribbon Microphone Usage (Royer R-121)

The Royer R-121 ribbon microphone is widely used in worship guitar recording:

- **Character:** Warm, natural sound without the hyped high-end of condenser mics. Captures the AC30 tone as it sounds in the room -- full-bodied and smooth.
- **SPL handling:** The R-121 handles over 135 dB SPL, making it usable on cranked tube amps unlike most vintage ribbon designs.
- **Typical positioning:** Placed slightly more on the dome (center) of the speaker, while the dynamic mic sits where the dome meets the paper cone.

### The SM57 + R-121 Combo (Industry Standard Dual-Mic Setup)

This is arguably the most popular guitar recording setup across genres, and worship is no exception:

- **Complementary characteristics:** The SM57 provides aggressive midrange and top-end bite. The R-121 provides body, warmth, and fullness. Blended together, they capture the complete frequency spectrum of the amp.
- **Phase alignment:** Critical when using two mics. The Royer AxeMount SM-21 mounting bracket positions both mics at optimal distances to maintain phase coherence.
- **Blend ratio:** Varies by song section -- more SM57 for cutting lead tones, more R-121 for warm rhythm tones. Many engineers start at 50/50 and adjust.

### Room Mics

Room mics are less commonly featured in modern worship guitar recordings compared to rock production. The trend toward tight, controlled mixes favors close-miking. However:

- Some producers use a room mic (large-diaphragm condenser, 6-15 feet back) blended in at low levels to add "air" and natural space
- Room sound is more important for live worship recordings (Hillsong Conference recordings, Elevation live records) where capturing the room energy is part of the production
- In studio overdub sessions, room mics are typically not used -- the space is added with reverb plugins

### The Fredman Technique

Two SM57s -- one straight on and one at a 45-degree angle with capsules positioned next to each other. This creates a frequency-cancellation pattern that naturally scoops certain midrange frequencies. Used occasionally in worship for heavier, more aggressive guitar tones.

## Mixing the Worship Guitar

### EQ Approach

Worship electric guitar EQ is primarily subtractive -- removing problem frequencies to make space for other instruments:

**Standard cuts:**
- **Below 80-100 Hz:** High-pass filter. Electric guitar has no useful information here. This range belongs to kick drum, bass guitar, and keys. For drop-tuned guitars, HPF at 60-70 Hz.
- **250-600 Hz:** Surgical cuts in this range to reduce muddiness, especially where guitar clashes with synths, keys, and lower vocal resonances. This is the "mud zone" where worship mixes get congested.
- **2-4 kHz:** Cut here to make room for the vocal. This is the most critical frequency range for vocal intelligibility. Even 2-3 dB of cut on guitar in this range dramatically improves vocal clarity.
- **5-6 kHz (low-pass on rhythm guitar):** Filtering out content above 5-6 kHz on rhythm guitar parts creates space for vocal "air," cymbal shimmer, and lead guitar brightness.

**Strategic boosts:**
- **1-2.5 kHz:** If guitar needs to push forward in the mix (lead lines, featured melodic parts), a gentle boost here adds presence without competing with the vocal's clarity range.
- **3.5-12 kHz:** Adds sparkle and brilliance for fingerpicking and clean arpeggiated parts.

**The frequency real estate map for worship:**
- **Below 100 Hz:** Kick and bass only
- **100-400 Hz:** Bass guitar body, guitar body (share carefully)
- **400-800 Hz:** Guitar and bass clash zone -- give bass priority below 600 Hz, guitar priority above 800 Hz
- **800-2 kHz:** Guitar owns this range for body and warmth
- **2-4 kHz:** Vocal priority zone -- guitar must yield
- **4-8 kHz:** Guitar presence and lead brightness, but share with cymbals
- **8 kHz+:** Air, shimmer -- shared between all elements

### Compression

**Individual guitar tracks:**
- Worship guitar is often lightly compressed or not compressed at all on individual tracks, relying on the amp's natural compression and the guitarist's dynamics
- When applied: ratio 2:1 to 4:1, slow attack (20-30ms to preserve pick transients), medium release
- Goal is to tame peaks, not reshape the dynamics -- worship guitar should breathe

**Guitar bus compression:**
- Route all guitar tracks to a guitar bus with a bus compressor
- Ratio: 2:1, catching 1-2 dB of gain reduction
- Attack: 20-30ms to retain punchiness
- A 2:1 ratio catching 1-2 dB can make a group of guitars feel like one instrument instead of six fighting each other
- This "glue compression" is standard on worship guitar buses

**Mix bus compression:**
- The SSL bus compressor (or emulation) is the worship mix standard
- 2:1 ratio, 3-4 dB of gain reduction maximum
- This is where "the magic glue starts happening"
- The Elevation Worship studio specifically calls out the SSL bus compressor as essential

### Reverb: Pedal vs. Mix

This is one of the most nuanced aspects of worship production:

**Pedal reverb (recorded in):**
- The guitarist's pedalboard reverb (Strymon BigSky, Boss RV-6, etc.) is typically recorded as part of the guitar signal
- This reverb is integral to the guitar's identity -- it is the tone, not added after
- Ambient parts, pad swells, and textural elements rely heavily on pedal reverb
- The challenge: once printed, pedal reverb cannot be adjusted in the mix

**Mix reverb (added by the producer/engineer):**
- Additional reverb applied in the DAW to place the guitar in a consistent "space" with other instruments
- Shorter, more subtle than pedal reverb -- room or plate settings that create cohesion
- Often has a high-pass filter (cutting below 200-300 Hz) and low-pass filter (cutting above 6-8 kHz) to prevent the reverb from muddying the low end or adding harshness
- A low-mid cut on reverb returns allows instruments and vocals to shine through cleanly while retaining the ambient effect

**The hybrid reality:** Most worship productions use both. The guitarist's pedal reverb provides the signature character and long tails. The mix engineer adds room/plate reverb for spatial placement and blend. The rule of thumb: the engineer keeps their hands off the long reverb (that is the guitarist's domain) and focuses on spatial placement with shorter, subtler verbs.

### Delay in the Mix

**Pedal delay vs. mix delay:**
- Dotted-eighth delay is almost always from the guitarist's pedalboard -- it is a performance technique, not a mix addition
- The mix engineer may add subtle slap delay (50-100ms) or short stereo delay for width on specific parts
- Longer delays in the mix are typically used on specific moments (a sustained note ringing into a chorus) rather than as a constant effect

**Delay settings reference (from the pedalboard, but informing preset design):**
- Dotted-eighth: The signature worship delay. Formula: (60,000 / BPM) x 0.75. At 120 BPM = 375ms. Creates the "galloping" rhythmic feel associated with The Edge and modern worship.
- Quarter note: (60,000 / BPM). At 120 BPM = 500ms. Used for more sustained, atmospheric passages.
- Feedback: 25% gives roughly 3 repeats, 50% gives roughly 6 repeats. Most worship applications stay below 50% feedback to avoid cluttering the mix.
- Mix level: Typically 20-35% wet for rhythmic delay, higher (40-60%) for ambient pads.

### Side-Chaining Guitar to Vocal

Yes, this technique is used in worship mixing:

- The vocal signal feeds the sidechain input of a compressor on the guitar bus
- When the vocalist sings, the guitar is gently ducked (1-2 dB of gain reduction)
- Settings: 2:1 ratio, threshold adjusted for 1-2 dB of reduction when vocals are present
- **Advanced approach:** Multiband sidechain compression across 800 Hz - 6 kHz keyed to the vocal. This ducks only the frequency range where guitar and vocal compete, leaving the guitar's low end and high shimmer untouched.
- This technique is more common in studio mixes than live sound, but some FOH engineers implement it on digital consoles

### The "Shimmer" -- Pedal vs. Plugin

Shimmer reverb (reverb with pitch-shifted octave-up content blended into the tail) is a defining worship guitar texture:

**How it works:** The reverb tail is sent through a pitch-shifter (typically octave up), then blended back into the reverb, creating a cascading, crystalline wash. A compressor before the pitch-shifter helps maintain density in the reverb tail.

**In production:**
- Most shimmer on worship records comes from the guitarist's pedals (Strymon BigSky, Eventide Space, TC Electronic HOF)
- Some producers add shimmer in the mix using plugins (Valhalla Shimmer is popular and affordable)
- The advantage of plugin shimmer: it does not disturb the reverb tail during editing. Pedal shimmer printed to the track makes editing difficult because cutting the audio also cuts the reverb tail.

**Preset design implication:** The shimmer effect should be available but controllable. Too much shimmer turns everything into an ambient wash with no definition.

### Low-End Management

Keeping guitar from clashing with bass and keys is critical in worship mixes:

- **High-pass filter at 80-100 Hz** on every electric guitar track (standard tuning). Drop tunings may go to 60-70 Hz.
- **400-800 Hz management:** This is where guitar and bass most frequently collide. Standard approach: bass priority below 600 Hz, guitar priority above 800 Hz, gradual crossover between.
- **Guitar owns the upper mids (1.5-3 kHz):** Do not boost bass here -- it will clash with guitars.
- **Complementary EQ:** When you boost one instrument in a frequency range, cut the other in the same range.
- **Keys/synths consideration:** Keys often occupy the same frequency territory as guitar (particularly pad sounds). Strategic EQ carving -- or arrangement decisions about who plays what register -- prevents the mix from turning to mud.

## Live Worship Sound Engineering

### The Silent Stage Revolution

**Timeline:** The silent stage concept gained mainstream adoption in worship settings around 2010-2015, driven by the availability of affordable digital mixers with personal monitor mixing and the maturation of amp modeling technology.

**What it means:** Instead of playing through on-stage amplifiers, guitarists go direct using modelers (Kemper, Fractal, Helix, etc.) or capture devices. Stage volume is eliminated or drastically reduced. Only vocals and acoustic drums (if not electronic) require microphones.

**How it changes guitar tone:**
- Eliminates the amp-speaker-room interaction that traditional guitar tone relies on
- The guitarist hears themselves entirely through in-ear monitors
- The FOH engineer receives a clean, direct signal with full control over the guitar tone in the house mix
- "Creates the ideal conditions for mixing live music" with greatly reduced feedback risk
- Enables clearer reproduction of stereo effects like shimmer reverb and ping-pong delays

**The tone trade-off:** Many guitarists feel that amp modeling through in-ears lacks the physical response and "feel" of a real amp pushing air. However, modern modelers have closed this gap significantly. The benefits for the congregation's listening experience (cleaner mix, better vocal intelligibility) typically outweigh the guitarist's preference for a live amp.

### In-Ear Monitors vs. Wedges

**In-ear monitor advantages:**
- Consistent mix regardless of stage position
- Individual mix control (musicians adjust their own monitor mix via apps on tablets/phones)
- Hearing protection at lower volumes
- No stage wash interfering with the house mix
- Stereo imaging -- guitarists can hear their delay and reverb in proper stereo

**Impact on playing:** Guitarists playing with in-ears often need to adjust their approach. Without the physical push of an amp, dynamics must be more intentional. The in-ear mix becomes the guitarist's entire sonic reality, making the quality of their monitor mix critical to their performance.

### Front-of-House Mixing for Worship Guitar

**EQ approach at FOH:**
- High-pass filter below 100-200 Hz to clear space for bass and kick
- Cut 2-4 kHz range on rhythm guitar to prioritize vocal intelligibility
- Cut 6-10 kHz on rhythm guitar so lead guitar can shine through
- Low-pass filter above 6 kHz so cymbals own the upper frequency space
- The 1-3.5 kHz range affects note definition -- boost for lead, cut for rhythm

**Handling multiple electric guitarists (2-3 players):**
- Assign clear roles: Rhythm (EG1) and Lead (EG2) parts designated before rehearsal
- EQ each guitarist differently: rhythm guitar gets more low-mid body, lead guitar gets more upper-mid presence
- Pan guitars slightly in the house mix (if the PA system supports stereo imaging)
- Avoid both guitarists playing similar parts in the same frequency space -- this is a arrangement/musicianship issue as much as a mixing issue
- Use arrangement to create differentiation: one guitarist plays clean ambient pads while the other plays driven rhythm

### Click Tracks and Backing Tracks

**The modern worship infrastructure:**
- Click tracks (metronome) sent to musicians' in-ears keep the band locked to tempo
- Backing tracks provide supplemental elements: synth pads, additional guitars, percussion, strings, vocal harmonies
- Popular platforms: Playback by MultiTracks, Prime by Loop Community, Ableton Live
- An audio interface connects the playback computer to the mixing console, with separate outputs for click (musicians only) and tracks (house mix)

**Pre-recorded guitar parts interacting with live guitar:**
- Standard MultiTracks stems include lead/backing vocals, acoustic guitar, two electric guitar parts (EG1, EG2), two keyboard parts, bass, drums, plus additional elements
- Many churches use backing tracks to supplement a smaller live team -- if you only have one guitarist, the backing track provides the second electric guitar part
- The live guitarist must be aware of what the backing track is providing to avoid doubling parts
- Some churches run everything on tracks with minimal live instrumentation; others use tracks only for supplemental texture (synth pads, click, guide cues)

**The implication for preset design:** Our presets should produce tones that sit well alongside backing track guitar stems, which are typically well-EQed, moderately compressed studio recordings.

## The Layers in a Worship Recording

### Case Study: "What A Beautiful Name" (Hillsong Worship)

Written by Brooke Ligertwood and Ben Fielding, produced by Michael Guy Chislett. Original key of D, 68 BPM.

**Estimated guitar layer breakdown** (based on album credits, MultiTracks stem structure, and production analysis):

1. **Acoustic guitar (foundation):** Strumming pattern providing harmonic and rhythmic foundation. Likely doubled (two takes, panned L/R) for width. Carries the song in the verse.

2. **Clean/edge-of-breakup electric (rhythm):** Open chord voicings with light drive. Enters in verse 2 or pre-chorus. Provides body and warmth underneath the vocal.

3. **Ambient electric (textural pad):** Volume swells, sustained chords with heavy reverb and delay. Creates the atmospheric bed. Panned wide using stereo effects.

4. **Driven electric (chorus rhythm):** More gain, fuller tone. Drives the energy of the chorus. Likely double-tracked and panned hard L/R for the chorus wall-of-sound.

5. **Lead electric (melodic):** Plays hooks, fills between vocal phrases, and the signature melodic moments. Panned center or slightly off-center. More presence in the 2-4 kHz range.

6. **Textural/ear candy:** Specific moments -- a harmonic here, a slide there, a filtered delay wash. These are the details that make a recording feel rich and alive.

**What makes it sound "big":**
- The transition from verse (acoustic + sparse electric) to chorus (full electric wall, doubled rhythms, ambient washes, lead melody) creates dynamic contrast
- Double-tracked rhythm guitars panned hard L/R in the chorus
- Ambient elements filling the stereo periphery
- Intentional frequency separation: each guitar occupies its own sonic lane
- Studio compression gluing the guitar bus together
- The SSL bus compressor tying the full mix into a cohesive whole

### Case Study: "Oceans (Where Feet May Fail)" (Hillsong United)

From the *Zion* album produced by Michael Guy Chislett. Key of D, 6/8 time.

**Estimated guitar layer breakdown:**

1. **Clean electric (primary):** The iconic clean arpeggiated pattern with dotted-eighth delay. This IS the song's guitar identity. Moderate reverb, precise delay timing.

2. **Acoustic guitar:** Gentle fingerpicking or light strumming providing harmonic support beneath the electric.

3. **Ambient electric (building):** Enters as the song builds. Volume swells and sustained notes creating an ocean-like wash of sound (appropriately). Heavy reverb, possibly shimmer.

4. **Driven electric (climax):** For the bridge/climax ("Spirit lead me where my trust is without borders"), a more driven tone enters with fuller chords, adding urgency and power.

5. **Lead/melodic electric:** Countermelody and fills, particularly in the build sections.

6. **Additional textural layers:** Reverse reverbs, filtered delays, harmonic swells that add movement and depth.

**What makes it sound "oceanic":**
- The 6/8 time signature creates a natural ebb and flow
- Dotted-eighth delay creates rhythmic complexity from simple note choices
- Layered reverbs that build from intimate to massive across the song's arc
- The guitar arrangement mirrors the ocean metaphor: calm, building, cresting, resolving

## The Gap Between Record and Live

### How Single Guitarists Reproduce What Is on the Record

The fundamental problem: a worship recording features 6-10 guitar tracks from multiple players. A typical church has one electric guitarist.

**Compromises single guitarists make:**
- Choose between rhythm (supporting the band) and lead (playing hooks/melodies) -- they cannot do both simultaneously
- Use effects (delay, reverb, shimmer) to fill sonic space that the record fills with multiple guitar layers
- Lean on backing tracks to provide the layers they cannot play live
- Use amp modelers with instant preset switching to jump between tones (clean verse to driven chorus) that would require different amps in the studio
- Accept that the live sound will be thinner and less complex than the recording

**Technology bridging the gap:**
- Digital modelers (Kemper, Fractal, Helix, Neural DSP) allow instant access to multiple amp tones and complex effect chains
- Preset systems like MainStage (Sunday Guitar templates) and modeler presets enable one-button switching between verse/chorus/bridge tones
- Backing tracks fill in supplemental guitar parts
- Stereo effects through a modeler into the PA system can create width that a single amp cannot

### How Multi-Guitarist Worship Teams Divide Responsibilities

The standard division for two electric guitarists:

**EG1 (Rhythm/Foundation):**
- Open chord voicings, strumming patterns
- Provides harmonic foundation when acoustic guitar is not present
- Lower gain, more body, occupies the low-mid frequency range
- Think: the part you would play if you were the only guitarist

**EG2 (Lead/Texture):**
- Melodic lines, fills, hooks between vocal phrases
- Ambient swells and textural elements
- Higher gain for lead moments, clean/ambient for pads
- Occupies the upper-mid frequency range
- Think: everything that makes the guitar sound interesting beyond just chords

**Critical rule:** Both guitarists should never play similar parts in the same frequency space simultaneously. If EG1 is strumming full chords, EG2 should be playing single-note lines or ambient pads -- not also strumming full chords.

## PRODUCTION-INFORMED PRESET DESIGN

These production insights should directly shape how we build Fader & Knob presets:

### EQ Curves That Sit in a Mix

Every preset should be designed with mix context in mind, not solo guitar tone:

- **Built-in high-pass behavior:** Our amp models should naturally roll off below 80-100 Hz. No preset should have significant energy below this point.
- **The 2-4 kHz vocal pocket:** Rhythm and ambient presets should have a gentle scoop (2-3 dB) in the 2-4 kHz range. This is where vocals live. Our presets should not fight the vocal.
- **Lead presets can fill the 2-4 kHz range** because lead guitar is meant to be heard when the vocal is not singing.
- **The 250-600 Hz mud check:** Every preset should be tested for excessive energy in this range. Worship mixes are dense with keys, synth pads, bass, and acoustic guitar all occupying this territory.
- **Upper presence (6-10 kHz):** Rhythm presets should roll off gently above 6 kHz. Lead presets can extend higher for cut and sparkle.

### Gain Levels That Leave Room for Vocals

- **Clean/ambient presets:** Minimal gain. The tone comes from effects (reverb, delay, modulation), not amp saturation. Think Fender-clean through an AC30.
- **Edge-of-breakup (the worship sweet spot):** Just enough gain that hard picking creates grit but soft picking stays clean. This dynamic response is what producers value -- it gives the guitarist control of intensity without changing presets.
- **Driven rhythm:** Moderate gain -- less than most guitarists think. Studio worship guitar is typically less distorted than live worship guitar because the studio mix has other elements providing density. Our presets should reflect studio gain levels, not "my bedroom amp sounds cool" gain levels.
- **Lead:** Moderate-to-high gain with sustain for melodic playing, but still less than rock/metal levels. Think Brian May, not Metallica.

### Delay Settings That Approximate Studio Treatment

- **Dotted-eighth delay should be available on every lead and rhythm preset.** This is non-negotiable for worship. It should be tempo-syncable.
- **Delay mix level:** 20-30% for rhythmic playing, 35-50% for ambient parts. Our presets should default to the lower end -- it is easier to add than subtract.
- **Delay feedback:** Default to 25-30% (3-4 repeats). This provides the rhythmic complexity without turning into an uncontrollable wash.
- **Tape/analog delay character:** Studio delay often has filtering (darker repeats that recede behind the dry signal). Our delay should not be pristine digital repeats at full bandwidth -- the repeats should lose some high end on each repetition.

### Reverb Settings That Approximate Studio Treatment

- **Reverb type:** Plate or hall for most applications. Spring reverb is not typical in worship.
- **Reverb pre-delay:** 20-40ms to keep the dry signal articulate before the reverb tail begins.
- **Reverb filtering:** High-pass the reverb return at 200-300 Hz (reverb should not add mud). Low-pass at 6-8 kHz (reverb should not add harshness or compete with vocal air).
- **Reverb mix level:** 15-25% for rhythm parts. 30-50% for ambient pads. Shimmer reverb should be usable but not defaulted to high levels.
- **Critical insight:** The reverb on the recording is a combination of the guitarist's pedal reverb (long, characterful) AND the mix engineer's spatial reverb (short, transparent). Our presets should capture the combined effect -- a sense of space without drowning the tone.

### The "Ready for Sunday" Test

Every preset should pass this test: if a worship leader hits play on a backing track and the guitarist plays through our preset, does it:

1. **Sit behind the vocal** without competing for attention?
2. **Fill appropriate frequency space** without creating mud or harshness?
3. **Respond to dynamics** -- does light picking sound clean and atmospheric while hard picking adds grit and urgency?
4. **Sound like it belongs on a recording** rather than a practice amp in a bedroom?
5. **Complement backing track guitar stems** without doubling their frequency content?

If a preset does all five, it bridges the gap between what is on the record and what a single guitarist can achieve live on a Sunday morning. That is the core value proposition.
