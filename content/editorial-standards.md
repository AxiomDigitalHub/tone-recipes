# Fader & Knob — Editorial Standards

This document governs the editorial quality, voice integrity, and publishing standards for every piece of content on Fader & Knob. It is the operating manual for maintaining a publication that is opinionated without being toxic, inclusive without being bland, and authoritative without being gatekept.

Every editor, writer (real or AI-generated persona), and contributor should internalize these standards. When in doubt, refer back here.

---

## 1. Brand Voice Foundation

### What Fader & Knob Is

Fader & Knob is a **tone publication**. Not a gear store. Not a forum. Not a YouTube channel transcribed into blog posts. It is an editorial authority on guitar tone — how to find it, shape it, understand it, and make it yours.

That distinction matters. A store's job is to sell you something. A forum's job is to host opinions. Our job is to **make you better at getting the sound in your head out of your speakers** (or headphones, or monitors, or PA system).

We publish with a point of view. We have writers who disagree with each other. We are comfortable saying a product is bad. We are equally comfortable saying a $100 pedal sounds better than a $400 one. The only loyalty we have is to the reader and the truth of what we hear.

### Core Belief

**Every guitarist deserves access to great tone, regardless of budget, platform, or genre.**

This is non-negotiable. It informs everything:

- We never write content that assumes the reader has money to burn.
- We never treat modelers as lesser instruments or tube amps as relics.
- We never frame one genre's tonal priorities as more valid than another's.
- We never assume the reader plays live, plays in a band, or plays for anyone other than themselves.

A parent playing through headphones for 20 minutes after bedtime deserves the same editorial care as a touring professional dialing in a festival rig. If a piece of content doesn't serve both, it needs revision.

### Opinionated vs. Elitist — The Line

This is the single most important editorial distinction we make. Fader & Knob writers have strong opinions. That is the point. But there is a hard line between "I think this sounds better" and "you're wrong for liking that."

**Opinionated (good):**
> "I've tried a dozen Klon clones and the Wampler Tumnus gets closest to the original's dynamic response. At $200, it's the one I'd buy." — Hank

> "Digital delay is objectively more reliable for live worship. I've had analog delays drift tempo mid-service and it's not a spiritual experience." — Nathan

> "Most metal players run too much gain. Drop it by 15-20% and your palm mutes will actually have definition." — Viktor

**Elitist (never):**
> "If you're playing through a Katana, you're not really playing guitar."

> "People who use amp sims don't understand what real tone is."

> "This pedal is for serious players, not beginners."

The test: **Does this statement help someone get a better sound, or does it make someone feel bad about their current setup?** If the latter, rewrite it.

### Relationship With Gear Manufacturers

Fader & Knob is editorially independent.

- We do not accept sponsored posts.
- We do not soften reviews in exchange for access, review units, or advertising relationships.
- We do not use affiliate links without disclosure (see Section 4 for affiliate link standards).
- We will say a product is bad if it is bad. We will say a cheap product beats an expensive one if it does.
- We will praise products from companies we personally dislike if the product deserves it.

When a manufacturer sends a review unit, we note it in the post: *"[Product] was provided by [Manufacturer] for review. Our editorial coverage is not influenced by review units."* This appears at the bottom of the post, not in the body.

If a manufacturer pulls access or complains about negative coverage, that is their right. We do not alter coverage in response. If anything, that kind of pressure makes us more thorough in our next review.

## 2. The Overton Window of Tone

Fader & Knob has 10 writers with wildly different perspectives. Rick thinks digital will never fully replicate a cranked tube amp. Sean thinks the tube-vs-digital debate was settled five years ago. Margot thinks your reverb choice reveals something about your soul. Elena thinks your reverb choice matters less than whether you can set it up before a toddler wakes up.

This diversity is the whole point. But diversity without a frame becomes chaos. The Overton Window defines the range of acceptable perspectives — the common ground every writer stands on, the territory they're free to fight over, and the positions that are out of bounds.

### What ALL Writers Must Agree On (Non-Negotiables)

These are the shared values of the publication. No writer, regardless of persona, may contradict these:

1. **Great tone is accessible.** You do not need expensive gear to sound good. Any content that implies otherwise fails editorial review.
2. **The player matters more than the gear.** Technique, taste, and practice always outweigh equipment. We sell knowledge, not GAS (Gear Acquisition Syndrome).
3. **Every platform is legitimate.** Tube amps, solid-state amps, modelers, plugins, amp sims in a DAW — all are valid tools. A writer can prefer one, but never dismiss another's legitimacy.
4. **Tone is subjective, but craft is not.** Whether a tone sounds "good" is personal. Whether a signal chain is well-structured, a gain stage is clean, or an EQ choice is intentional — those have right and wrong answers.
5. **The reader's time is sacred.** Every post must deliver actionable value. We do not publish filler.
6. **Respect for all skill levels.** A beginner asking "what does gain do?" is not less worthy of our attention than a touring pro asking about parallel routing in a Quad Cortex.
7. **Honesty above access.** We never soften a take to protect a manufacturer relationship, please a readership segment, or avoid controversy.

### What Writers CAN Disagree On (Legitimate Territory)

These are the productive disagreements that make the publication interesting:

- **Analog vs. digital superiority** — Rick can insist tubes have "something" digital can't replicate. Sean can argue that's confirmation bias. Both positions are welcome as long as both acknowledge the other platform makes great music.
- **Complexity vs. simplicity** — Sean's 47-block Helix preset and Carl's Telecaster-into-a-Blues-Jr are both valid approaches to tone. Neither is objectively right.
- **Budget thresholds** — Jess thinks $200 is the sweet spot. Margot thinks $400 boutique pedals justify their price. Both can make their case.
- **The role of effects** — Nathan sees effects as atmospheric tools that serve the song. Dev sees effects as compositional instruments. Margot sees effects as tonal seasoning. All valid.
- **How much gain is enough** — Viktor's disciplined metal gain staging vs. Rick's "crank it and let the tubes do the work" are both legitimate philosophies.
- **New gear vs. vintage gear** — Hank's appreciation for vintage circuits and Sean's enthusiasm for new technology can coexist.
- **Practice vs. gear as the solution** — Carl's "stop buying pedals and practice" vs. Elena's "the right gear makes practice more inviting" are both defensible.
- **Live tone vs. recorded tone** — Dev and Viktor can argue that these are fundamentally different goals. Jess and Rick can argue that what sounds good sounds good everywhere.

### What NO Writer Should Ever Say (Out of Bounds)

These positions damage the brand and are rejected at editorial review:

- **Gear shaming.** "If you're playing through [budget product], why bother?" or "That's a toy, not an instrument." Never. Not even as a joke.
- **Genre dismissal.** "Worship guitar isn't real guitar." "Metal players don't have taste." "Lo-fi is just an excuse for bad tone." Every genre has legitimate tonal goals.
- **Demographic dismissal.** "This isn't for bedroom players." "Serious musicians don't use headphone amps." If it alienates a reader for who they are rather than challenging what they know, it's out.
- **Manufacturer sycophancy.** "Line 6 can do no wrong." "Everything Boss makes is cheap garbage." We evaluate products individually, not by brand loyalty.
- **False authority claims.** "As a professional, I can tell you..." used to shut down discussion rather than share expertise. Credentials support arguments; they don't replace them.
- **Absolutism without nuance.** "There is literally no reason to own a tube amp in 2026." "Digital will never sound as good." Absolute statements close conversations. Our job is to open them.

### Inside the Window vs. Outside the Window — Examples

**Inside the window:**
> "I've A/B'd the Revv G20 against its Neural DSP capture, and through studio monitors at moderate volume, I genuinely cannot tell them apart. At a gig pushing air through a 4x12? Different story. The physical cab interaction matters, and no IR has fully nailed it yet." — Rick

This is opinionated, specific, acknowledges nuance, and invites further conversation.

> "The Behringer SF300 is a $25 pedal that sounds like a $150 fuzz. The build quality is what you'd expect at that price — plastic housing, questionable switch longevity — but the circuit itself delivers. If you're on a budget, this is a no-brainer." — Jess

This is honest about both strengths and weaknesses. It respects the reader's budget without pretending the product is flawless.

**Outside the window:**
> "Anyone still buying tube amps is just paying for nostalgia. The technology is dead. Move on."

Even Sean, our digital advocate, wouldn't say this. He'd say modelers have won on convenience, cost, and consistency — but he respects that some players value the tube amp experience. The technology isn't dead; the trade-offs have shifted.

> "I can't recommend any amp under $800. Below that price point, you're just getting noise."

This contradicts our core belief. The Boss Katana, Fender Mustang, and dozens of other sub-$400 amps produce genuinely excellent tone. Pretending otherwise alienates most of our readership.

> "This worship stuff is all just delay with reverb. It's not real playing."

Genre dismissal. Nathan's atmospheric approach requires as much skill, taste, and intentionality as Rick's blues leads. Different skills. Equal legitimacy.

### How to Handle Genuinely Bad Products

Some products are bad. We don't pretend otherwise, but we follow principles:

1. **Be specific about what's bad.** Not "this sucks" but "the high-gain models have an unpleasant digital fizz above 4kHz that no amount of EQ taming resolved" or "the footswitches failed after three weeks of normal use."
2. **Acknowledge what it gets right, if anything.** "The clean models are serviceable, and the reverbs are surprisingly usable. But the drives — which are the reason most people would buy this unit — don't hold up."
3. **Compare to alternatives at the same price.** A $150 pedal isn't bad because it doesn't match a $500 one. It's bad if a different $150 pedal does the same job better.
4. **Consider the intended audience.** A beginner multi-effects unit that sounds "meh" to Margot might be perfect for a 14-year-old getting their first pedalboard. Write for the actual buyer.
5. **Never be cruel.** Someone designed this. Someone is proud of it. We can be honest without being contemptuous. "This doesn't meet the standard set by its competitors at this price" is direct and fair. "I don't know how this made it past quality control" is needlessly hostile.
6. **Name what would fix it.** When possible, identify what a firmware update, a component change, or a different design choice would solve. This shows we're engaging seriously, not dunking.

## 3. Quality Gates (Editing Protocol)

Every post must pass all ten quality gates before publication. An editor works through these sequentially. If a post fails any gate, it returns to the writer (or editorial) for revision. A post does not skip a gate because it passed the others well.

### Gate 1: Factual Accuracy

- [ ] All gear model names are spelled correctly and use the manufacturer's official naming (e.g., "HX Stomp" not "Helix Stomp," "Quad Cortex" not "QC" in first reference)
- [ ] Year and model references are verified (e.g., "The TS808 was introduced in 1979" — not 1978, not 1980)
- [ ] Signal chain claims are technically accurate (e.g., if a post says "put the compressor before the drive," the reasoning must be correct)
- [ ] Knob positions, parameter values, and settings are realistic and testable (e.g., "Drive at 3, Tone at 5, Level at 7" should produce the described sound)
- [ ] Frequency references are correct (e.g., "the midrange hump around 720 Hz" for a Tube Screamer is accurate)
- [ ] Price references include a "street price as of [date]" qualifier — prices shift, and stale numbers erode trust
- [ ] Any claim about how a product works (DSP processing, analog circuit behavior, digital conversion) is verifiable

**Common factual errors to watch for:**
- Confusing the TS808 and TS9 circuits (different clipping diodes, different output section)
- Getting Helix model names wrong (there are dozens and they update)
- Attributing a guitarist's tone to the wrong piece of gear (SRV used Tube Screamers, not Klon — the Klon didn't exist until '94)
- Misquoting impedance values, wattage ratings, or speaker configurations

### Gate 2: Persona Subtlety Check

The most important voice gate. Could a reader enjoy this article without knowing which writer wrote it? If the article only works because of the byline, it needs revision.

- [ ] **The 80/20 rule passes.** 80% of the article is about the subject. No more than 20% is writer-colored perspective.
- [ ] **Self-reference count is 0-2.** The writer references their own identity, lifestyle, background, or signature perspective no more than twice per 1,000 words. Count them. If it's more, cut.
- [ ] **Signature phrases appear at most once.** If a phrase is in the writer's signature list, it can appear once per article. Some articles should have zero. Catchphrases are callbacks, not refrains.
- [ ] **The opener is about the subject.** The first paragraph should hook the reader into the topic. The writer's personality shows in *how* they approach it, not in announcing who they are. Rick doesn't open by telling you he's an analog guy. He opens about the subject, and you can tell he's Rick by how fast he gets to the point and what detail he leads with.
- [ ] **The closing varies.** Signature signoff style in no more than 1 in 4 articles. Other closings should end with: a specific recommendation, a final observation about the subject, a forward-looking thought, or simply the last useful point.
- [ ] **Persona is implicit, not performed.** The writer's identity comes through in what they notice, what they compare things to, what they skip, and what they assume the reader knows — not in self-description or lifestyle announcements.
- [ ] **Forbidden-word avoidance isn't conspicuous.** If the absence of a word feels performed (like someone carefully not saying "workflow"), the voice needs loosening. Natural absence, not performed avoidance.

**Persona subtlety red flags:**
- The writer mentions their own gear, background, or playing situation in more than two paragraphs
- Signature phrases appear more than once
- The opening paragraph is about the writer rather than the topic
- Disagreements with other writers are explicitly staged ("I respect X, but...")
- The article reads like a character monologue rather than a person writing about guitar tone

### Gate 3: Voice Consistency

- [ ] The post reads like the attributed writer wrote it — sentence length, vocabulary, humor style, and technical depth match the writer's profile
- [ ] The writer's perspective is consistent with their previous posts (Rick doesn't suddenly love menu-diving; Elena doesn't suddenly recommend a 45-minute setup routine)
- [ ] The writer's technical depth matches their profile (Carl shouldn't be explaining parallel routing; Viktor shouldn't be glossing over gain staging)

**Voice consistency red flags:**
- A paragraph that could have been written by any writer (too generic)
- Vocabulary that belongs to a different writer (Dev using "crank it" or Rick using "sits in the mix")
- Emotional register that doesn't match (Carl being effusive, Viktor being casual, Nathan being cynical)
- Technical depth that's too high or too low for the attributed writer

### Gate 4: Reader Knowledge Baseline

Fader & Knob assumes its reader: owns a guitar, has been playing for at least a year, knows what gain/overdrive/reverb/delay are, understands the basic concept of a signal chain, and has at least looked at a pedalboard.

**What we do NOT define:** Gear categories (overdrive, delay, reverb, compressor), signal chain basics, or widely-known gear (Tube Screamer, Big Muff, Boss DD series, Strymon). First-reference rules apply to specific models, not to categories.

**What we DO define on first use:** Specialized terms (impulse response, buffer, gain staging, sag, DSP), platform-specific jargon (HX Edit, Cortex Cloud, BOSS Tone Studio), and anything that requires knowledge beyond "I play guitar and have a few pedals."

- [ ] The post doesn't over-explain basics that the reader already knows. Defining what an overdrive does, explaining what a signal chain is, or telling the reader a Tube Screamer is popular are all AI tells. Cut them.
- [ ] Specialized terms are explained naturally on first use, not condescendingly
- [ ] Acronyms are spelled out on first reference (IR = impulse response, DAW = digital audio workstation)
- [ ] The level of assumed knowledge is appropriate for the post's category
- [ ] The post feels like it was written for a community of players, not for someone who wandered in off the street

### Gate 5: Surprised Discovery Requirement

Every gear-related article must include at least one moment where the writer's expectation was wrong or their assumption was challenged. This signals genuine experience rather than reported specification.

Template: "I expected [X]. What I found was [Y]. Here's why that matters."

- [ ] The article includes at least one "surprised discovery" — a moment of revised expectation, a contradiction, or an unexpected finding
- [ ] The discovery is specific and testable, not vague ("I was surprised by how good it sounded" fails; "I expected the low end to get muddy below the 5th fret, but the note separation actually held up better than my TS808" passes)
- [ ] The article contains at least one genuine hypothesis-testing moment, not just conclusions delivered with confidence

### Gate 6: Tone Description Specificity

Generic tone descriptors are banned without qualifiers. "Warm," "musical," "transparent," "lush," and "rich" used alone are meaningless. They must be followed by a specific qualifier.

- [ ] Every tone description includes at least one of:
  - A comparison to a specific, named sound (a song, an album, a player's tone in a particular era)
  - A physical/spatial metaphor that's specific ("the reverb makes the guitar feel like it's in a tiled bathroom with one window open" not "the reverb makes the guitar sound bigger")
  - A contradiction ("it's aggressive but polite about it")
  - A negative-space description ("there's no fizz above 4kHz, which is where most high-gain pedals fall apart")
- [ ] No instance of "warm," "musical," "transparent," "lush," or "rich" appears without a specific qualifier following it
- [ ] Tone descriptions read like someone who played the thing, not someone who read about it

**Good:** "It sounds like a small amp being angry." / "The kind of crunch where the speaker is about to give up but doesn't." / "An amalgam of trashy punk spittle, string detail, and an imploding Fender tweed."

**Bad:** "Warm overdrive with musical compression." / "Tight low-end with defined pick attack." / "Rich, lush reverb tails."

### Gate 7: Actionability

- [ ] The reader leaves with something they can **do** — a setting to try, a signal chain to build, a product to investigate, a technique to practice
- [ ] Abstract claims are grounded in specific recommendations ("dynamics matter" is a thesis; "try rolling your guitar volume to 7 for the verse and 10 for the chorus" is actionable)
- [ ] Settings references are precise enough to reproduce (not "set the drive to medium" but "Drive at 4-5 on a 0-10 scale")
- [ ] Comparison posts conclude with a clear recommendation or decision framework, not "it depends on what you like" (the reader already knows that — tell them *how* to decide)
- [ ] Every post answers the question: "What should I do differently after reading this?"

**Actionability failure examples:**
- A 1,500-word post about reverb types that never tells you which to try first
- An amp comparison that ends with "both are great, it's up to you" instead of "if you prioritize X, buy A; if you prioritize Y, buy B"
- A tone guide that describes a sound without giving the settings to achieve it

### Gate 8: Tone Calibration

- [ ] The post is opinionated without being hostile
- [ ] The post is helpful without being condescending
- [ ] The post is confident without being arrogant
- [ ] Humor is present but never punches down (at budget players, beginners, specific genres, or non-professional musicians)
- [ ] Negative assessments are specific, fair, and constructive (see Section 2, "How to Handle Genuinely Bad Products")
- [ ] The post doesn't read like marketing copy — no breathless superlatives, no "this changes everything," no "you NEED this"
- [ ] The post doesn't read like a textbook — dry, authoritative, and lifeless. Personality should be present on every page.

**Calibration spectrum:**

Too cold: "The Strymon Flint provides reverb and tremolo in a single enclosure. It has three reverb modes and three tremolo modes."

Too hot: "The Strymon Flint is absolutely INSANE. If you don't own one, what are you even doing??"

Calibrated: "The Strymon Flint does two things — reverb and tremolo — and does them well enough that it's earned a permanent spot on my board. The '70s spring reverb mode alone is worth the price." — Nathan

### Gate 9: SEO Hygiene

- [ ] Meta description is 150-160 characters and includes the primary keyword naturally
- [ ] The title is compelling, specific, and includes the primary keyword (not "Thoughts on Reverb" but "Spring vs. Plate vs. Hall Reverb: Which One Belongs on Your Board?")
- [ ] Heading hierarchy is correct (one H1 via the title, H2 for major sections, H3 for subsections — no skipped levels)
- [ ] At least 2-3 internal links to related recipes, gear pages, or other blog posts
- [ ] Primary keyword appears in the first 100 words of the body
- [ ] Image alt text is descriptive and includes relevant keywords where natural
- [ ] URL slug is clean, lowercase, hyphenated, and keyword-relevant (e.g., `/blog/tube-screamer-settings-guide`)
- [ ] No keyword stuffing — if a phrase appears more than 3-4 times per 1,000 words, it's probably forced

### Gate 10: Cross-Platform Fairness

- [ ] If the post discusses a technique or tone goal, it includes guidance for at least two platforms (e.g., analog pedalboard AND modeler, or Helix AND Quad Cortex)
- [ ] No post implies that one modeler brand is the "default" or "best" without specific justification for that claim in that context
- [ ] Budget options are represented alongside premium options
- [ ] Headphone/bedroom players are considered alongside gigging players (where relevant to the topic)
- [ ] The post doesn't assume the reader owns specific gear — settings are described generically enough to translate ("a Tube Screamer or similar mid-hump overdrive" rather than only "a TS808")

## 4. Content Formatting Standards

### Blog Post Structure Template

Every post follows a rhythm. Not a rigid formula — but a recognizable structure that readers can rely on.

**Intro (100-200 words)**
- **The first paragraph is about the topic, not the writer.** Hook the reader into the subject. The writer's personality shows in *how* they approach it — what they notice, how fast they move, what they assume you know — not in announcing who they are.
- Promise a clear deliverable: "Here are the exact settings..." or "By the end of this post, you'll know how to..."
- No throat-clearing. The first sentence should earn the second sentence.
- Do NOT use the intro to establish the writer's identity, lifestyle, or signature perspective. No "As someone who's been gigging since..." or "It was 2 AM in my bedroom..." unless the anecdote directly serves the topic.

Bad intro: "Today we're going to talk about overdrive pedals. There are many types of overdrive pedals available..."

Also bad: "I've been playing through tube amps since 1978, so trust me when I say..." (the writer performs their identity instead of earning it through the writing)

Good intro: "You've got a Tube Screamer. Everybody's got a Tube Screamer. But I'd bet most of you are running it with the drive at noon and wondering why it sounds like a wet blanket. Here's the thing — the TS808 is five different pedals depending on how you set it." (You can tell this is Rick by the directness and the assumption of shared experience, without him telling you)

**Body sections (200-400 words each)**
- Each section should be a self-contained idea with its own subheading
- Open with the key point, then support with detail and examples
- Include specific, testable settings or actionable steps where relevant
- Close each section with a transition to the next or a micro-takeaway

**Closing (100-200 words)**
- Summarize the actionable takeaway (not a recap of every section — the single most important thing)
- **Vary closings.** Only 1 in 4 articles should use the writer's signature closing style. The other 3 should end with: a specific recommendation, a final observation about the subject, a forward-looking thought, or simply the last useful point. The closing should feel like the end of a conversation, not the end of a performance.
- Do not end with "Happy playing!" or any generic sign-off
- The best closings are about the subject, not the byline

### Heading Hierarchy Rules

- **H1:** Title only (set in frontmatter, not in the body)
- **H2:** Major sections. Should be scannable — a reader skimming only H2s should understand the post's structure.
- **H3:** Subsections within an H2. Used for individual settings, comparisons, or steps within a broader section.
- **H4:** Rare. Only for sub-subsections in very long technical posts. If you need H4 frequently, the post may need restructuring.
- Never skip levels (no H2 directly to H4).
- Headings should be descriptive, not clever. "How to Set Up a Clean Boost" beats "The Secret Sauce."

### How to Reference Gear Settings

Consistency in settings notation prevents confusion.

**Knob positions — prefer clock positions over numbers:**
> Drive: about 2 o'clock | Tone: noon | Level: around 3 o'clock

Clock positions feel like real playing experience. Use "about" or "around" before values — real players don't think in precise numbers. Include what happens if you go too far: "Push the drive past 3 o'clock and it starts to get fizzy" tells the reader more than "Drive: 7/10."

Always include context: the guitar, pickup position, and volume setting that produces the described sound. "With the bridge humbucker, volume at full, drive at about 1 o'clock" is a complete picture.

**Numerical scale (0-10) when needed:**
> Drive: 3-4 | Tone: 5 | Level: 7-8

Use a 0-10 scale when the gear has numbered markings or when precision matters. For percentage-based controls (most modelers), use the percentage:
> Gain: 35% | Bass: 50% | Mid: 65% | Treble: 55% | Master: 40%

**Modeler parameters:**
> Amp Model: US Double Nrm (Helix) / Fender Twin (QC equivalent)
> Gain: 4.5 | Bass: 5.0 | Mid: 6.5 | Treble: 5.5 | Master: 7.0 | Sag: 3.0

Always identify the specific amp model name as it appears in the modeler's interface, followed by the real-world amp it's based on if not obvious.

**Clock positions** (when the gear has no numbered markings):
> Volume: 2 o'clock | Tone: noon | Gain: 10 o'clock

Noon = straight up. Use o'clock notation for pedals with unmarked knobs.

**Toggle/switch positions:**
> Clipping mode: Symmetrical (switch position 1 of 3)

### Image and Media Guidelines

- Every post should include at least one supporting image (signal chain diagram, knob position photo, or gear layout)
- Images must have descriptive alt text: not `alt="pedal"` but `alt="Tube Screamer knob positions set for clean boost: Drive at 1, Tone at 5, Level at 9"`
- Signal chain diagrams should read left-to-right (input to output)
- Do not use manufacturer press photos without permission. Prefer original photography or clearly licensed stock
- Embedded audio/video clips are encouraged for tone comparisons — let the reader hear the difference, don't just describe it

### Internal Linking Requirements

- Every post must link to at least 2 other Fader & Knob posts or recipe pages
- Links should be contextually relevant, not forced (link "Tube Screamer" to the Tube Screamer settings guide when it's mentioned naturally)
- Use descriptive anchor text: "our [guide to signal chain order](/blog/signal-chain-order-guide)" not "click [here](/blog/signal-chain-order-guide)"
- Reciprocal linking: when publishing a new post, update 1-2 older related posts to link back to the new one

### Word Count Guidelines by Post Type

| Post Type | Minimum | Target | Maximum |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tone recipe (single sound) | 400 | 600-800 | 1,000 |
| Settings guide (one pedal/amp) | 800 | 1,200-1,500 | 2,000 |
| Gear comparison (A vs. B) | 1,000 | 1,500-2,000 | 2,500 |
| Deep dive / explainer | 1,200 | 2,000-2,500 | 3,500 |
| Beginner guide | 800 | 1,200-1,500 | 2,000 |
| Opinion / perspective piece | 600 | 800-1,200 | 1,500 |
| Crossover / dual-writer piece | 1,200 | 1,800-2,500 | 3,000 |

Under the minimum means the post doesn't have enough substance. Over the maximum means it should probably be split into two posts or tightened significantly.

### Affiliate Links and Product Recommendations

Fader & Knob may use affiliate links. The rules are absolute:

1. **Disclose clearly.** Every post with affiliate links includes the following at the top, below the byline: *"This post contains affiliate links. If you purchase through these links, Fader & Knob earns a small commission at no extra cost to you. This does not influence our editorial recommendations."*
2. **Never recommend a product because it has an affiliate program.** The recommendation comes first. The link follows. If we'd recommend it without the affiliate relationship, it gets a link. If we wouldn't, it doesn't.
3. **Always include a non-affiliate alternative.** If we link to a product on one retailer, mention that it's also available elsewhere. The reader should never feel trapped.
4. **Don't over-link.** One affiliate link per product mention is sufficient. Repeating the same link in every paragraph is aggressive and damages trust.
5. **Affiliate links never appear in negative reviews.** If we don't recommend a product, we don't monetize traffic to it.

## 5. Writer Interaction Rules

### Handling Writer-vs.-Writer Disagreements

**The core rule: writers never explicitly name other staff writers to set up a disagreement.** No "I respect Rick, but..." or "Sean makes a fair point, however..." These are polite, structured, and completely unlike how real musicians argue about gear.

Disagreements should emerge organically across articles. If Nathan writes "The BigSky is worth every penny for what it does to a room," and Jess writes in a different article "I sold my $500 reverb in three months. One good algorithm is all you need," the disagreement is alive and real without either of them performing it.

**In crossover posts** (two writers, one topic), the same principle applies with more structure:

1. **Each writer states their position with confidence.** No diplomatic preambles. No "I see where X is coming from." Just say what you think.
2. **Disagree on specifics, not character.** "Digital delay loses something in the tape saturation" is a specific claim. "He's stuck in the past" is a personal attack.
3. **Let the reader hold both positions.** Don't resolve the tension. Real disagreements in music don't have winners. The reader is smart enough to decide for themselves.

### When to Defer to Another Writer's Expertise

Writers have domains. Respect them:

- **Vintage gear history and circuit analysis:** Hank is the authority. If Rick wants to discuss a vintage Fuzz Face circuit, Hank should review that section for accuracy.
- **Modeler signal flow and DSP:** Sean is the authority. If Viktor is writing about high-gain modeling, Sean should review the technical claims.
- **Live worship context:** Nathan is the authority. No writer should opine on what works in a church service without Nathan's input.
- **Recording and production context:** Dev is the authority on how guitar sits in a mix.
- **Budget and accessibility:** Jess and Elena are the authorities on whether something is genuinely affordable and practical.
- **Metal-specific gain staging:** Viktor is the authority. His is the final word on noise gates, low-tuning EQ, and pick attack dynamics.
- **Vintage market values and authenticity:** Hank. Period.

This doesn't mean other writers can't touch these topics. It means they should have the relevant expert review their claims before publishing.

### Maintaining Character Consistency Over Time

A writer persona should evolve, but never contradict itself without narrative justification.

**Evolution (good):**
Rick started deeply skeptical of modelers. Over 20 posts, he's grudgingly admitted that the Helix's Fender models are "close enough for club work." This is natural growth — he's had more experience, his ears have been challenged, and he's honest enough to update his priors. A reader who's followed Rick can see the arc and it feels earned.

**Contradiction (bad):**
Rick publishes a post raving about going fully digital and selling all his amps. This contradicts everything established about his character. Unless there's a compelling narrative reason (and even then, it needs editorial discussion), this breaks reader trust.

**Guidelines for character evolution:**
- Changes in opinion happen slowly and are acknowledged in the text ("I never thought I'd say this, but...")
- Core traits remain stable (Carl will always prefer simplicity; Margot will always care about nuance)
- If a writer encounters something that genuinely challenges their worldview, make it a story. That's great content.
- Keep a running document of each writer's established positions so new posts don't accidentally contradict old ones

### Introducing a New Writer

If a new persona is added to the roster:

1. **Introduction post.** The writer's first appearance is a standalone "meet the writer" piece or a topic post with a clear editorial note: *"Introducing [Name], Fader & Knob's voice on [domain]."*
2. **Establish the voice immediately.** The first post must be unmistakably different from existing writers. If readers can't tell the new voice apart within two paragraphs, the persona needs more differentiation.
3. **Define the gap they fill.** Why does this writer exist? What perspective was missing? The answer shouldn't be "we needed more content" — it should be "no one on our roster speaks to [audience/genre/approach]."
4. **Crossover within the first month.** Pair the new writer with an established one early. This introduces the new voice to the audience through a familiar one and immediately establishes where the new writer fits in the ensemble.

## 6. Content Calendar Framework

### Post Frequency Targets

| Cadence | Minimum | Target | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Blog posts per week | 2 | 3-4 | Mix of long-form and shorter recipe-style posts |
| Tone recipes per week | 1 | 2-3 | These are the core product — specific, actionable tone setups |
| Deep dives per month | 2 | 3-4 | Longer explainers, gear comparisons, technique breakdowns |
| Crossover posts per month | 1 | 2 | Two writers, one topic — these drive engagement |
| Opinion/perspective per month | 1 | 2-3 | Writer-specific takes, "hot take" pieces, editorial voice content |

### Balancing Writer Representation

No single writer should dominate the publication. Guidelines:

- **No writer publishes more than 25% of total content in any given month.** If you're publishing 12 posts/month, no writer appears more than 3 times.
- **Every writer publishes at least once per month.** If a writer goes silent for a full month, readers lose connection with that voice.
- **Rotate the "featured" position.** Each month, one writer gets a longer spotlight piece (deep dive, major review, or perspective essay). Rotate this across the roster.
- **Track representation quarterly.** Review the content calendar every quarter to ensure no writer has been over- or under-represented. Adjust the next quarter's plan accordingly.

### Seasonal and Timely Content

Certain events and seasons create content opportunities. Plan for them:

**Annual tentpoles:**
- **January (NAMM):** New gear announcements, first impressions, "what we're excited about" roundups. Assign by specialty — Sean covers new modelers, Hank covers reissues, Viktor covers new high-gain amps, etc.
- **Black Friday / Holiday season (November-December):** Gift guides by budget tier, "best deals" roundups, "what to buy a guitarist" posts aimed at non-guitarist shoppers (Elena is ideal for this — she speaks to a non-obsessive audience)
- **Back-to-school (August-September):** Beginner-focused content, "your first pedalboard" guides, college dorm setup posts (headphone-focused)
- **Summer (June-August):** Outdoor gig and festival content, pedalboard reliability guides, "survive a hot stage" posts

**Event-driven content:**
- When a major modeler firmware update drops (Helix, QC, TONEX), publish a first-impressions piece within 72 hours
- When a significant new product launches in our space, assign a review and a "should you care?" quick-take
- When a major artist's rig gets public attention (rig rundowns, live footage), tie into it with a "how to get that tone" post

**Timely content lead times:**
- NAMM and Black Friday content should be outlined 3-4 weeks before the event
- Firmware/product reaction pieces should publish within 1-3 days of the announcement
- Seasonal content (summer gigging, holiday gifts) should publish 2-3 weeks before the season peaks

### Evergreen vs. Timely Content Ratio

**Target ratio: 70% evergreen / 30% timely.**

Evergreen content (signal chain guides, technique explainers, genre tone breakdowns) drives long-term search traffic and remains useful for years. Timely content (gear announcements, firmware reactions, seasonal guides) drives short-term engagement and keeps the site feeling alive.

If the ratio drifts past 50/50 toward timely content, the site starts to feel like a news blog. If it drifts past 85/15 toward evergreen, it starts to feel stale. Monitor and adjust monthly.

## 7. Review and Revision Process

### The Three-Stage Review

Every post passes through three stages before publishing.

**Stage 1: Self-Review (Writer)**

The writer (or the person generating the writer's content) reviews the draft against these questions:

- Does this sound like the attributed writer? Read it aloud. Does it flow the way this person speaks?
- Is there a clear, actionable takeaway? Can the reader do something with this information today?
- Are all gear references accurate? Model names, settings, signal chain claims?
- Is this the right length? Does every paragraph earn its place?
- Would I share this with a friend who plays guitar? Does it clear the "is this actually useful?" bar?

**Stage 2: Editorial Review (Editor)**

The editor runs the post through all seven Quality Gates (Section 3). In addition:

- Flag any claim that feels unverifiable or suspiciously specific
- Check that the post doesn't duplicate a topic already covered without adding a new angle
- Verify that internal links are functional and point to relevant content
- Ensure the post fits into the current content calendar without creating redundancy
- Confirm that the word count is appropriate for the post type

**Stage 3: Brand Voice Check (Final)**

A final read focused exclusively on voice and brand:

- Does this post uphold the Overton Window? Would any line make a reader feel excluded?
- Does the writer's voice ring true? Are there paragraphs that sound generic or "off"?
- Does the post, taken alongside recent publications, maintain the diversity of perspective we promise?
- Would you be proud to put the Fader & Knob name on this?

### Common Revision Patterns

These are the issues that come up most often. Know them and you'll write cleaner first drafts.

**"Too long, lost the thread."**
The post wanders. There's good material buried in there, but the structure has drifted. The fix is usually to identify the single core argument, cut sections that don't support it, and tighten transitions. A 2,500-word post that should be 1,500 words is not a longer post — it's a worse post.

**"Too much jargon, no on-ramp."**
The post assumes the reader already knows what terms mean. The fix is not to dumb everything down — it's to provide brief, natural context on first use. "Set the sag parameter (which controls how much the virtual power supply compresses under load, mimicking a tired tube amp)..." is accessible without being condescending.

**"Lost the character's voice."**
The post reads like a generic gear blog. The fix: go back to the writer's profile. What words would they use? What would they never say? What's their sentence rhythm? Rewrite the opening paragraph in deep character, then let that energy carry through the rest.

Example revision note: "This section reads like Sean wrote it — too precise, too clinical. Rick wouldn't say 'the algorithm interpolates between cab models.' Rick would say 'it blends two speaker sounds together, and honestly, it does it pretty well.' Same information, Rick's voice."

**"No actionable takeaway."**
The post describes and explains but never tells the reader what to do. The fix: add specific settings, a recommended signal chain, a product to try, or a technique to practice. Every section should pass the "so what do I do with this?" test.

**"Too hot / too mean."**
A negative review crossed the line from honest to hostile. The fix: keep the assessment, change the tone. "This pedal has no reason to exist" becomes "At this price point, the [competitor] offers better build quality and a more usable EQ range. It's hard to recommend the [product] when the alternative is this strong."

**"Reads like marketing."**
Breathless superlatives, every feature is "incredible," no criticism anywhere. The fix: find something honest to say that isn't pure praise. Even a product you love has trade-offs. "The Strymon Timeline is the most versatile delay on the market" becomes "The Strymon Timeline does more than you'll ever need — which is both its strength and its complexity tax. If you want 200 delay options, it's unbeatable. If you want to plug in and play, you'll spend your first weekend in menus."

### Giving Feedback in Character Context

When editing posts written by a fictional persona, feedback should reference the character, not just the prose quality. This keeps the voice consistent and makes the revision direction clearer.

**Instead of:** "This paragraph is too wordy."
**Say:** "Carl wouldn't use this many words. His whole thing is brevity. Cut this to two sentences."

**Instead of:** "The tone is too negative here."
**Say:** "Jess is sarcastic, not bitter. She'd roast this pedal with a joke, not a lecture. Try: 'Honestly, for this price, I expected it to at least stay in tune.'"

**Instead of:** "This section needs more technical depth."
**Say:** "Viktor would go deeper here. He'd want to know the exact frequency where the fizz lives and what EQ cut fixes it. Give me the numbers."

**Instead of:** "The closing is weak."
**Say:** "Nathan always closes with something warm and encouraging — 'try this at your next rehearsal and see how the room responds.' This closing just... stops. Give it a Nathan ending."

**Instead of:** "This doesn't feel authentic."
**Say:** "Hank tells stories. This reads like a spec sheet. Where's the part where he bought this pedal at a swap meet in 1997 and it changed how he thought about fuzz? Give me the Hank version."

This approach keeps the revision process collaborative and character-driven, producing better results than abstract prose criticism.

## 8. AI Content Generation Protocol

When using AI to generate content in a writer's voice, follow this process. The order matters.

### Step 1: Subject First, Voice Second
Before writing, ask: "What is this article about?" not "Who is writing this article?" Write the article first for content and accuracy. Then do a voice pass to adjust sentence rhythm, vocabulary, and comparison points to match the writer.

### Step 2: The Voice Pass
During the voice pass, adjust:
- **Sentence rhythm and length** to match the writer's patterns
- **Comparison points** — what does this writer reach for? (Rick reaches for classic amps; Sean reaches for A/B data; Margot reaches for sensory metaphor)
- **What gets skipped** — remove explanations the writer wouldn't bother with
- **Assumed knowledge** — adjust what's defined vs. taken for granted

### Step 3: The Subtlety Audit
After writing, count and verify:
- How many times does the writer's identity show explicitly? (Mentioning their own background, values, gear, or situation.) **Target: 0-2 per article.** If more, cut.
- Does this article include at least one moment of genuine surprise, contradiction, or revised expectation?
- Are tone descriptions specific (named comparisons, spatial metaphors, contradictions) or generic (warm, musical, transparent)?
- Could someone enjoy this article without knowing who wrote it? If the article only works because of the byline, revise.
- Does the opener serve the reader or introduce the character?

### The North Star
The best guitar journalism reads like you're sitting next to someone at a gig who happens to know a lot about gear. They're not introducing themselves. They're not performing a character. They're just talking about the thing in front of them, and who they are comes through in how they talk about it.

## Appendix: Quick Reference

### The Five Questions Every Post Must Answer

1. **Who is this for?** (Beginner? Intermediate? Genre-specific? Platform-specific?)
2. **What will they learn?** (A technique? A setting? A decision framework? A new perspective?)
3. **What can they do after reading?** (Specific action, not vague inspiration)
4. **Who is saying it?** (Which writer, and does their voice come through clearly?)
5. **Does it uphold the brand?** (Inclusive, honest, opinionated, never elitist)

### The One-Sentence Brand Test

If you're unsure whether a piece of content belongs on Fader & Knob, apply this filter:

*Does this help a guitarist get closer to the sound they want, written in a voice they trust, without making them feel bad about where they're starting from?*

If yes, publish. If no, revise until the answer is yes.
