## A Tone Recipe Is a Complete Signal Chain

Think of it like a cooking recipe, but for guitar tone. Instead of ingredients and steps for a dish, a tone recipe gives you every block in your signal chain -- from your guitar's pickup selector to the final output -- with specific settings for each one.

A tone recipe includes:

- **The amp model** and its exact settings (gain, bass, mid, treble, presence, volume)
- **Every effect in the chain** -- drive pedals, modulation, delay, reverb -- with knob values
- **Signal chain order** -- what goes where and why it matters
- **The context** -- what song, what part of the song, and what makes this tone tick

No vague advice like "add some delay." Instead, you get something like: Digital Delay, 380ms, 30% mix, 2 repeats. That's the difference between a tip and a recipe. One is a shrug. The other is an answer.

## Why Recipes Beat Presets

Honestly, presets are black boxes. You download one, load it up, and it either sounds right on your setup or it doesn't. And it usually doesn't -- because your guitar, pickups, and monitoring are different from whoever made the preset. Now you're stuck tweaking blind.

A tone recipe is transparent. You can see every setting, understand why each block is there, and adjust intelligently. If your pickups are hotter than the recipe assumes, you know to back off the drive a notch. If you're playing through studio monitors instead of a cab, you can compensate. If you're running a Jazzmaster through a Big Muff into your modeler (no judgment), you know exactly which parameters to nudge.

Recipes teach you how tone works. Presets just give you a file. Nobody in the crowd cares about your preset library -- they care about whether you sound good.
