## You Don't Need a Giant Pedalboard

There are too many pedals in the world.

You see boards with 20 or 30 pedals on them. That's not for you. Not yet. Maybe not ever.

Most recorded guitar tones come from three to five things in a row. Learn these three chains. They cover almost everything.

Works the same with real pedals or modeler blocks.

## Chain 1: The Clean Machine

**Guitar → Compressor → Amp (clean) → Reverb**

This is your clean tone. Country, jazz, funk, R&B. Anything where the note needs to ring out clear.

### Why This Order

Compressor goes first. It evens out your picking before the signal hits the amp. Quiet notes come up. Loud notes come down.

Amp stays clean. No breakup. Just volume and whatever EQ character it has.

Reverb goes last. It adds space to the clean signal. That's all it needs to do.

### What Each Effect Does

- **Compressor:** Squeezes your dynamic range. Notes sustain longer. Picking sounds more even. Set it subtle. You shouldn't hear it working. You should feel it.
- **Reverb:** Simulates a room or a hall. Without it, clean guitar sounds dry. Short room reverb for funk and country. Longer hall reverb for ambient stuff.

### Try It On

Think of a Mayer clean tone. Compressed Fender clean with a touch of reverb. It just works.

## Chain 2: The Crunch Machine

**Guitar → Overdrive → Amp (edge of breakup) → Delay → Reverb**

Blues, classic rock, indie. Anything warm and crunchy that responds to your hands.

### Why This Order

Overdrive goes before the amp. It pushes the front end harder. Adds harmonics and midrange. Like turning the amp up without touching the volume knob.

Delay comes after the amp. You want it to repeat the dirty signal cleanly. Delay before dirt makes a mess.

Reverb goes last. Puts the whole thing in a room. Sounds natural.

### What Each Effect Does

- **Overdrive:** Warm, natural distortion that responds to your pick. Pick light and it cleans up. Dig in and it crunches. That dynamic response is the whole point.
- **Delay:** Repeats your signal after a set time. One slapback repeat at 100-200ms adds depth. Start with 2-3 repeats at 350ms for a good rock delay.
- **Reverb:** Same as Chain 1 but use less of it. Too much reverb with overdrive gets washy fast.

### Try It On

Keith Richards. Angus Young. Gary Clark Jr. The details change. The structure doesn't. Drive into amp. Time effects after.

## Chain 3: The High-Gain Machine

**Guitar → Noise Gate → Distortion → Amp (high gain) → Delay → Reverb**

Metal. Hard rock. Heavy stuff. Tight and aggressive.

### Why This Order

Noise gate goes first. High gain amplifies everything, including noise. The gate silences your signal when you stop playing. Without it, every pause fills with hiss.

Distortion goes before the amp. Heavy, saturated gain. Some players use the amp's own high-gain channel instead. Some stack both.

Delay and reverb go after the gain stage. Same logic as Chain 2. Keep them short and low in the mix for heavy tones. Subtle delay adds depth. Short room reverb gives it life.

### What Each Effect Does

- **Noise Gate:** Cuts your signal below a threshold. Playing, gate opens. Stop, gate closes. Silence. Set the threshold high enough to kill noise but low enough to let your sustain breathe.
- **Distortion:** Heavy, saturated clipping with lots of sustain. Unlike overdrive, it doesn't clean up much when you back off your volume. It stays aggressive.

### Try It On

Metallica. Foo Fighters. Any modern heavy tone. The gain level and EQ change. The chain stays the same.

## The Common Thread

Same pattern in all three chains. Gain-shaping effects before the amp. Time-based effects after it.

That's the rule. It works whether you run three pedals or thirty.

Once you know these three chains, you can start adding. Wah before the overdrive. Chorus between the amp and delay. But build from here first. A simple chain you understand will always sound better than a complicated one you don't.

The guitar's waiting.
