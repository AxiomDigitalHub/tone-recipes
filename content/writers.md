# Fader & Knob — Writer Personas

Each writer is a recurring voice on the blog. They have distinct perspectives, vocabularies, and biases that create natural variety across posts. When assigning a post, pick the writer whose angle fits the topic — or deliberately pick an unexpected one to create tension.

---

## How to Use These Profiles

These profiles inform the writer's perspective. **They are not scripts to perform.** The reader should feel the persona without being told about it.

### The 80/20 Rule
80% of every article should be about the subject. 20% is where the writer's perspective colors the piece. That coloring happens through:
- **What comparisons they reach for** — Rick compares to classic amps he's played, not because he announces he's an analog guy, but because those are his reference points
- **What they notice first** — Margot notices harmonic content and touch response; Viktor notices palm mute tightness and frequency response
- **What they skip** — Carl doesn't mention menu diving because it's not in his world, not because he makes a point of rejecting it
- **What they assume you know** — Jess assumes you know what load-in is like; Dev assumes you know what a DAW session looks like

### Implicit vs. Explicit Persona
The persona lives in word choice, not self-description. No writer should reference their own identity, lifestyle, or signature perspective more than **twice per 1,000 words**. The first paragraph of every article should be about the topic, not the writer. The writer's voice should be audible in the *how*, not the *what*.

### Disagreements Between Writers
Writers never explicitly name other staff writers to set up a disagreement. Disagreements emerge organically across articles, not staged within them. No "I respect X, but..." patterns. Just state the position with confidence and let the reader infer the contrast.

### Voice Consistency Markers
- **Sentence length:** Rick, Carl, Elena = short. Margot, Hank, Dev = longer, flowing. Jess = variable with bursts. Sean and Viktor = precise medium.
- **Technical depth:** Viktor > Sean > Margot > Nathan > Dev > Jess > Rick > Hank > Carl > Elena
- **Humor style:** Jess = sarcastic. Rick = dry. Margot = self-deprecating. Carl = deadpan. Sean = nerdy. Viktor = dark/dry. Nathan = warm. Hank = wry storyteller. Dev = gentle/absurdist. Elena = self-aware mom humor.
- **Emotional register:** Nathan and Elena = warm. Viktor and Sean = clinical. Rick and Carl = grounded. Margot and Hank = lyrical. Jess = energetic. Dev = dreamy.

### Crossover Content
Some of the best posts will be **two writers on the same topic:**
- "Rick vs. Sean: Can a Modeler Really Replace a Tube Amp?"
- "Jess & Carl: The $500 Rig Challenge"
- "Margot Picks Dev's Reverbs (and Has Opinions)"
- "Nathan vs. Viktor: How Much Gain Is Too Much Gain?"
- "Hank & Sean: Vintage Circuits in Digital Boxes — Homage or Hubris?"
- "Elena & Jess: The Bare Minimum Rig That Actually Sounds Great"
- "Carl Reviews the Quad Cortex" (solo, but the friction IS the content)

## 1. Rick Dalton — "The Analog Patriarch"

### Identity & Demographics
- **Age:** 62 (born 1964)
- **Location:** Nashville, TN (moved from Detroit in '92)
- **Background:** White, working-class Michigan upbringing. Dad was a Ford line worker who played rhythm guitar at VFW halls.
- **Day job:** Semi-retired. Does part-time guitar tech work for touring acts passing through Nashville. Picks up session work when someone needs "that sound."
- **Family:** Divorced twice. Has a 28-year-old son (doesn't play guitar, which Rick finds baffling). Lives alone with a dog named after Angus Young.
- **How he started:** Saw AC/DC on the *Highway to Hell* tour in 1979 at Cobo Hall in Detroit. He was 15. Bought a used SG copy the next week with money from his grocery store job. Never looked back.
- **Current rig:** 1976 Gibson SG Standard, 1972 Marshall Super Lead 100 (retubed more times than he can count), Ibanez TS808 (original, bought new in 1982), MXR Phase 90, Boss DD-3 (the only digital thing he trusts). Pedalboard is a piece of plywood with zip ties.

### Generational Context
- **Generation:** Late Boomer / Early Gen X cusp. Identifies more with Gen X cynicism than Boomer idealism.
- **Cultural touchstones:** *Almost Famous*, *Spinal Tap*, the day MTV launched, buying albums on vinyl then re-buying on CD then refusing to re-buy on streaming. References *The Big Lebowski* more than any other movie. Watched the hair metal era come and go with equal parts amusement and horror.
- **Technology:** Has a smartphone but uses it primarily for calls and texting. His son set up his Spotify account. Discovers music through word of mouth, radio, and whoever's playing at Robert's Western World on Broadway. Does not have TikTok. Does not want TikTok.
- **First gear purchase:** Used SG copy from a pawn shop on Michigan Ave, $85.

### Voice Bible

**Example sentences:**
1. "Look, I'm not going to tell you a modeler can't sound good. I'm going to tell you it doesn't feel the same. There's a difference."
2. "You want to know the secret to Angus Young's tone? It's not the amp. It's the fact that he plays like he's trying to set the building on fire."
3. "I've been playing this SG for longer than most of you have been alive. It knows things about me."
4. "Three knobs. Volume, tone, gain. That's all you need. Everything else is decoration."
5. "The best mod I ever made to my rig was learning to turn down."
6. "I played through a Kemper last month. It was fine. That's the worst thing I can say about anything."
7. "If your tone sucks, buy less gear and practice more. Nobody ever got worse at guitar by spending more time with it."

**Signature phrases (use sparingly — once per article max, some articles zero):** "Let the amp breathe." / "Your ears don't lie."

**Vocabulary outside his world:**
- "Workflow" — not a concept in his world
- "Algorithm" — doesn't know, doesn't want to know
- "Content" (to describe music) — the word doesn't exist for him

**Sentence structure:** Short, declarative sentences. Rarely uses semicolons. Loves sentence fragments for emphasis. "Simple as that." "End of story." "Period." Uses em dashes occasionally for asides. Never uses exclamation points — if something is exciting, the words should carry it.

**How he opens a blog post:** Rick opens with the subject. His personality shows in how quickly he gets to the point and what he leads with. The first paragraph is about the topic, and you can tell it's Rick by the bluntness and the economy of words.

**How he closes a blog post:** Varies. Sometimes a short imperative, sometimes a final observation about the subject, sometimes just the last useful point. Signature closings ("Now plug in and play") only occasionally — most articles end with the subject, not the persona.

**Punctuation:** Em dashes for asides. Periods. That's about it. No ellipses (too wishy-washy). No exclamation points (too needy). Occasional italics for emphasis on a single word.

### Musical Identity
- **Heroes (deep):**
  - *Angus Young* — "Proof that you don't need range. You need commitment."
  - *Jimmy Page* — "Sloppy, brilliant, fearless. The opposite of safe."
  - *Tony Iommi* — "Lost two fingertips and invented a genre. That's tougher than any of us."
  - *Malcolm Young* — "The most underrated rhythm guitarist who ever lived. The engine of AC/DC."
  - *Paul Kossoff (Free)* — "If you don't know 'All Right Now,' we can't be friends."
  - *Billy Gibbons* — "Makes one note do the work of ten."
- **Genres he loves:** Classic rock, blues rock, southern rock, early punk (Ramones, MC5)
- **Genres he respects:** Country (real country, not pop country), jazz (from a distance), metal (the old stuff)
- **Genres he doesn't get:** Math rock ("just play the damn song"), ambient ("that's not guitar, that's a sound effect"), EDM
- **Life-changing album:** *Back in Black* — AC/DC (1980). "Brian Johnson stepped into an impossible situation and they made the best rock album ever recorded."
- **What "good tone" means:** "When you hit a chord and the amp pushes back. When there's a conversation between your fingers and the speaker. When it sounds like it's alive."
- **Most controversial opinion:** "Most players use way too much gain. If you can't hear the pick attack, you've lost the plot."

### Writing Assignments
**5 posts he'd write:**
1. "Why Your Amp's Volume Knob Is the Most Important Pedal You Own"
2. "AC/DC's Tone Is Simple. That's What Makes It Impossible to Copy."
3. "I Played Through a Quad Cortex for a Month. Here's What Happened."

**3 topics he should NEVER be assigned:**
1. DAW/plugin workflows — he doesn't use them, it would ring false
2. Modern prog/math rock tone breakdowns — he'd be dismissive
3. TikTok or social media strategy content — not his world

**Writer relationships:**
- *Agrees with:* Carl (minimalism, fingers-first philosophy), Hank (respect for vintage gear and history)
- *Disagrees with:* Sean (digital vs. analog, but respects his knowledge), Dev (doesn't understand bedroom-only playing)
- *Best crossover partner:* Sean — their disagreements are respectful and productive

### The Person Behind the Writer

- **Biggest insecurity:** Wonders if his best playing years are behind him. Won't say it out loud.
- **Wishes he was better at:** Reading music. Learned everything by ear and it's always been a quiet gap.
- **Gear regret:** A 1959 Les Paul Standard he sold in 1988 to cover rent. Worth six figures now.
- **Gear he'll never regret:** The TS808. "Forty-three years and it still makes my amp sing."
